Ladies and Gentlemen,

I thank you for having responded so numerous to the invitation of the Government of Finland to hold the 7th OIML Conference and, at the same time, a meeting of our International Committee, here in Helsinki.

During the opening session of the Conference, this afternoon, I will present a report on the activities for the period 1980-1984.

This allows me now to be very brief and to limit this opening address only to those points which concern the Committee. First of all, and according to tradition, I would like to greet our new Colleagues on the Committee.

A new State has joined this year, namely Brazil, and its representative is Mr. Walter dos Santos, President of the Metrology Institute of Brazil, INMETRO.

Welcome, Mr dos Santos! We already had occasions to meet, CGPM, ILAC, and I am very happy that you participate also in the activities of OIML.

Several States have changed their representatives since our 19th meeting. These are:

- Austria, Mr Lewisch replacing Mr Rotter,

- Dem. People's Rep. of Korea, the representative being now Mr Kim Hi Sang,

- Cuba, represented by Mr Gomez Rosell who was rencently designated as President of the OIML Development Council,

- Spain, represented by Mr Cadarso,

- Ethiopia, by Mr Afework,

- France, by Mr Bertran,

- Ireland, by Mr McGrath,

- — Sri Lanka, by Mr Madanayake,

- Tunisia, by Mr Ben Gaid,

- Venezuela, by Mr Reyes Cabrera.

Concerning Denmark, it is to day that Mr Repstorff Holtveg is handing over his duties to Mr Petersen.

Concerning the German Democratic Republic we have received from Mr Liers a personal message which the Director of the Bureau will now read:

Mr President,

Messrs Vice-Presidents,

Ladies and Gentlemen, Members of the Committee,

Mr Director,

On the occasion of the 20th CIML meeting, I am sending to you my greetings.

Having been made responsible by my Country, the German Democratic Republic, for the cooperation with OIML, since my participation at the First General Conference of Legal Metrology in 1956, and as Member of CIML and the Presidential Council, I have always followed the development of our International Organization and, as far as it was possible for me, have tried to promote this development.

To my very great regret my health made it necessary for me to cease my professional activities which for almost 37 years were concerned with legal metrology.

It is with great pleasure and gratitude that I recall our working together, between colleagues, working for many years with objectivity and mutual respect.

I allow myself to express the hope that you will accept my successor with the friendliness I have experienced myself.

I take this opportunity to say goodbye to you adding my wishes for your good health and well being.

May the work of national and international metrology be always successful. Even if, from now on, I will be retired I shall follow the developments in metrology with the greatest interest and, in particular, of our International Organization of Legal Metrology.

Yours sincerely,

Hans Werner Liers

September 1984.

I will answer our colleague, in the name of all of you; in the meantime not having yet received the official notification from the Government of the German Democratic Republic, we shall consider that for this meeting Mr Liers is still a Member of the Committee and is represented here by Mr Damm.

Finally, concerning Switzerland, Mr Koch will replace Mr Perlstain as from 1st January 1985.

I welcome all our new Colleagues and during the coming week I will have the pleasure of personal contacts with all of them here present.

I come now to the work program of our 20th meeting. In principle, there are two sessions : one this morning, another Friday afternoon. However, should it be necessary it will be possible to organize an additional session either Tuesday or Thursday, late afternoon, after the meeting of the Conference.

This morning we shall examine mainly the agenda and the organization of the Conference and then we shall discuss some of the problems which will be considered also by the Conference. One of the most important questions will certainly be the long-term planning of OIML work. We shall devote to this problem all the necessary time so that clear and constructive decisions can be taken by the Committee and I can then report to the Conference.

In our Agenda of the Committee there are furthermore several problems which can be considered independently of the Conference, that is to say, either today or on Friday.

Finally, Friday afternoon, we shall have to examine the conclusions and decisions of the Conference in order to implement them.

That is, Ladies and Gentlemen, my dear Colleagues, the work program which I propose to you. I would ask the Director of the Bureau to proceed with the roll-call of delegates and to give us all the information we may require.

We shall then examine the Agenda and you may make any proposals you consider useful.

We can now open our discussions.

I thank you in advance for your participation in our work.

## 3. EXAMINATION OF THE DETAILED DRAFT REGULATIONS FOR THE OIML RETIREMENT PLAN

Mr BIRKELAND recalls that the principle of the creation of an OIML retirement plan was the subject of a decision taken by the 6th Conference. Subsequently, during its 19th meeting, the Committee has accepted the expert's report on the cost of this retirement plan, which confirmed the estimates presented to the 6th Conference. Complying with a request of the Committee, the Bureau has then prepared a draft of the detailed rules of the retirement plan, which was submitted to the meeting for study.

Mr EDGERLY points out that the Authorities concerned, of his Country, have examined the draft very carefully and have found it acceptable with the exception of the use of certain terms. The Bureau takes note of the requested editorial modifications.

Mr AMODEO presents the comments of the Italian Authorities :

the retirement plan should not result in an increase of the contributions of the Member States,

the contributions of the BIML staff members should be increased from 6 to 9 % of salary,

- the right to a pension should be granted after at least 10 years of service with BIML and not after more than 5 years.

## M. ATHANE replies that :

- a 10 % increase of the contributions of the Member States spread over the years from 1992 to 2009 has already been considered acceptable by the Conference,

- the figure of 6 % fixed by the Conference as a contribution to the retirement plan by BIML staff member could be reviewed periodically, to take account of the figures in force in the French Administration (at present 7 %) and in other International Institutions; this problem will be reviewed during the 8th Conference in 1988,

- there have been many discussions within the Committee on the minimum duration of service qualifying for a pension ; « at least 5 years » was considered acceptable taking into account the length of BIML contracts which is 5 years ; thus to be granted the right to a pension, a staff member has to obtain a renewal of this contract which means that in many cases he will have worked for BIML at least 10 years.

Mr AMODEO takes note of these explanations and indicates that the Italian Delegation will present the same comments to the Conference.

With reference to a question by Mr NDOUGOU, Mr ATHANE confirms that the retirement plan will not result in an increase of the subscriptions before 1992 in fact for the staff members concerned, the plan replaces the former system Gratuity upon termination of service paid from the provident fund the maintenance of which is already included in the OIML budget. For a number of years therefore, the pensions can be paid from this provident fund without the need for any additional budgetary provisions.

At the end of these discussions, the Committee adopts (with abstention of Italy) the following resolution:

The International Committee of Legal Metrology

ADOPTS the detailed rules of the OIML retirement plan

INSTRUCTS its President and the Director of the Bureau to present at the appropriate time a report on the implementation of these rules with the aim of possible modifications or improvements.

## 4. EXAMINATION OF PROPOSALS OF THE PRESIDENTIAL COUNCIL CONCERNING LONG-TERM PLANNING OF OIML WORK, AND THE ROLE AND RESPONSIBILITIES OF CIML MEMBERS

Mr BIRKELAND presents the document on planning, elaborated by the Presidential Council according to the decisions of the Committee (18th and 19th meetings) and distributed to Committee Members in June 1984.

The aim of this work is to give a better structure to the work of OIML Secretariats, to define the priorities, to make better use of the results of the work of other International Institutions, to cause interest of the greatest number of National bodies and thus to create new resources; all this in order to be able to take on the ever increasing work load for which OIML is responsible.

To this end the Presidential Council has tried to cover all the fields in which measurements are of interest to governments (for reasons of commercial equity, health and environment, etc.) in order to define the fields of legal metrology activities.

The submitted document should enable the Secretariats, with guidance from the Committee, to direct their work according to established priorities and to coordinate it within the organization (liaison between OIML Secretariats) as well as outside it (liaison with working groups of other institutions).

From the practical point of view, this document proposes a planning cycle for the period 1985-1988 initially and should be repeated in 4-years periods between Conferences, each new planning period taking into account the results achieved during the preceding period.

Mr ISSAEV who with Mr Edgerly was the principal author of this document recalls the difficulties which were met ; he underlines also that the work was done with the aim of a better redistribution of responsibilities between the Presidential Council, Committee Members and Pilot or Reporting Secretariats.

Mr. EDGERLY points out the differences which exist between the two matrices attached to the document : the one is intended to draw attention to the ever increasing role played by measurement in the economy of a country and to define the practical limits of legal metrology ; this matrix is presented for information and possible use by the Members of the Committee ; the other is a working tool for the Pilot and Reporting Secretariats permitting them to achieve the objectives listed by Mr BIRKELAND.

Mr PETERSON indicates that the Danish Delegation has studied this document with great interest and has found it positive and most useful. In particular, the care with which the co-operation with other Institutions was studied is very much appreciated. However, the direct use of the matrices is not evident and additional explanations or detailed advice would be required.

Mr ATHANE replies that in fact the implementation of this document will reveal any shortcomings and that the Bureau is available to help the Secretariats any experience gained will be taken advantage of during future planning periods.

Mr SINHA expresses the support of India for the work done. He has however three comments, as follows:

- in the fields in which OIML and other Institutions (such as ISO or IEC) are active at the same time, note should be taken of the difference between the objectives of these various Institutions; for example, at ISO there are a few « product standards » but many « test standards 」; one should be careful about the harmonization of these test standards with the OIML metrological requirements; if necessary, the modalities of the co-operation between OIML, ISO, IEC, etc., should be reviewed,

- research and development constitute a field of vital importance ; is it right that this field should be outside the sphere of OIML responsibility ?

- advice should be provided for the Developing Countries in order to help them in the formulation of their legislation.

Mr BIJLOO supports the document but points out some errors or omissions concerning the field of activity of SP2-Sr6 (electronics).

Mr PODGORSKI also expresses his support. However, the Polish Delegation has identified some editorial inconsistencies which must be corrected.

Messrs GOMEZ ROSELL and NDOUGOU give in turn their full support. Mr Ndougou however comments that the classic techniques should not be forgotten because of their continued importance for the Developing Countries.

Mr BIRKELAND requests the Bureau to introduce in the final document all the suggested corrections.

At his suggestion, the Committee adopts the following resolution :

The International Committee of Legal Metrology

ACCEPTS the report concerning long-term planning presented by the OIML Presidential Council,

REQUESTS the Pilot and Reporting Secretariats to implement the conclusions of this report,

INSTRUCTS the Presidential Council to monitor the implementation,

INSTRUCTS its President to report on the implementation during the next meeting of the Committee and requests him to inform the 7th Conference about the decisions taken on this subject.

## 7. EXAMINATION OF THE SITUATION OF SOME SECRETARIATS

## 7.1. SP5 (volumes of liquids)

The Committee accepts the proposal presented by the Fed. Rep. of Germany, United States and France to split into two SP5, a part « static measurement » under the responsibility of France and a part « dynamic measurement » under that of the Fed. Rep. of Germany.

In answer to a question of Mr ISSAEV it was stated that certain Reporting Secretariats attached to SP 5S (in particular SP 5S-Sr1 and 2) concern also SP 5D and that appropriate liaison should be established.

## 7.2. SP13 (electricity)

Mr THULIN presents a study prepared by the Bureau about the possible activity of this Pilot Secretariat in liaison with IEC work. For the majority of the subjects it would be ideal for OIML not to undertake any specific work but to influence the corresponding IEC work so that the IEC publications (which, in general, cover the specifications as well as the test methods) are suitable for legal metrology; the OIML Recommendations could then be limited to a selection of necessary specifications and tests within the framework of legal metrology, as it was done with sound level meters.

Mr PERLSTAIN endorses this point of view and asks whether one could not simply abolish all the Reporting Secretariats attached to SP13 and set up a liaison body with IEC which, in certain cases, would prepare the supplements necessary for the implementation of IEC publications by the National Services of Legal Metrology.

Mr. EDGERLY, responsible for SP13, and Mr BIRKELAND conclude stating that no hasty decision should be taken on this subject; the SP13 will examine these proposals when establishing its 1985-1988 work program.

## 7.3. Photometry and radiometry

Mr. THULIN recalls that in April 1984 the Bureau carried out an enquiry in order to find out the interest shown by the Member States in these subjects; this enquiry included extracts from IEC and CIE $ ^{*} $ work programs.

By October 1984 the Bureau had received 9 replies, 4 favourable, 3 negative and 2 with no opinion, which was not sufficient to take a decision.

Mr EDGERLY indicates that he could agree to the creation of such Secretariat provided that its field of work is clearly defined and corresponds to the needs of legal metrology.

Mr PETERSEN is, more or less, of the same opinion although he has doubts about the actual need of dealing with these very technical and scientific subjects within the framework of legal metrology.

Mr OHLON considers that there is very little legal need for them and suggests that OIML should concentrate its resources on more important subjects.

Mr MUHE states that the legal aspects of photometry and radiometry mainly concern the protection of the environment and medicine : the Pilot Secretariats SP17 and SP26 could therefore examine the interest that exists for such studies without it being necessary to create a new Secretariat.

Mr EDGERLY agrees with what was just said by Mr MUHE ; however, he does not agree with certain other statements since in the USA, as no doubt in many countries, the problems of lighting in medicine, safety at work, etc., have clearly legal aspects.

Mr THULIN indicates that, in the opinion of the Bureau, the OIML work should be carried out with reference to the IEC and CIE work and would therefore not represent an excessive workload.

Mr IIZUKA declares that originally he had the same opinion as Mr Ohlon ; however the statements of Messrs Mühe and Edgerly have caused him to delay taking a stand. He will write to the Bureau on this subject at some later date, if necessary.

Mr BIRKELAND concludes, requesting the Bureau to continue studying the situation and to try, in liaison with the interested countries, to draw up a work program for both photometry and radiometry.

## 7.4. SP 17-Sr 3 (radioactive pollution)

Mr ATHANE points out that the United Kingdom having initially accepted responsibility for this Reporting Secretariat was obliged to abandon it; another candidate country must therefore be found.

Concerning a question put by Mr MUHE, Messrs ZSDANSZKY and THULIN discuss the risks of duplication between SP16 and SP17-Sr3 ; in the opinion of Mr Zsdanszky the SP16-Sr1 and 2 work only on dosimetry whilst SP17-Sr3 would work on the measurement of radionuclide activity ; Mr Thulin however notes that SP17-Sr3 has in its work program the personal dosemeters.

Mr BIRKELAND requests that, if necesary, this question be resolved when the 1985-1988 work programs are established and that any candidate country for the Secretariat should make itself known to the Bureau. Mr MUHE indicates that even if his country cannot accept responsibility for SP17-Sr3 it can participate actively and provide the legal requirements already in force.

## 7.5. SP 2-Sr 4 (recognition of metrological controls)

Mr BIRKELAND gives a summary of the discussions which took place during the Conference and considers that it is not necessary to repeat them. In conclusion, the Committee adopts the following resolution:

## The International Committee of Legal Metrology

HAVING TAKEN NOTE of the report of the SP 2-Sr 4 meeting in March 1984,

CONSIDERS that the actions undertaken or planned by SP 2-Sr 4 conform with the work program of this Secretariat,

ENCOURAGES SP2-Sr4 to actively continue its work,

CONFIRMS that, in the long run, the ultimate aim of the SP2-Sr4 work is the creation of an OIML certification system,

CONSIDERING that the success of the SP2-Sr4 work and, in the long run, the creation of an OIML certification system depend largely on the activities of the other OIML Secretariats,

REMINDS the Pilot and Reporting Secretariats that they must elaborate harmonized test methods for the instruments for which they are responsible,

ENCOURAGES the Pilot Secretariat SP22 to actively continue the work concerning, in particular, the principles of pattern evaluation and of verification of instruments.

Mr ISSAEV wishes that the Committee reconsiders the resolution adopted on this subject in 1983, in order to state precisely that mutual recognition is not only concerned with the test results but also with the methods of testing and of verification $ ^{*} $ .

Mr BIRKELAND declares that it is impossible to modify a resolution already adopted but states that the proposal of Mr Issaev is in fact applied in the documents of SP 2-Sr 4.

## 7.6. Suppression of certain Secretariats

Following the proposal of the Bureau and of the States concerned, the Committee suppresses the following three Reporting Secretariats :

SP 9-Sr4 Surface tension of liquids used for calibration of hydrometers.

SP 13-Sr 1 International compatibility of national primary standards used for the verification of instruments.

## SP 19-Sr1 International force comparison.

Mr PERLSTAIN requests that the decisions, repeatedly taken and confirmed, concerning the suppression of inactive Secretariats or of those which are no longer of any interest to legal metrology should be implemented.

Mr BIRKELAND replies that this will in effect result from the long-term planning.

## 7.7. SP 2-Sr 6 and SP 7-Sr 2

Mr PETERSEN underlines the extreme urgency of the work of both these Secretariats and suggests that the Committee recognise this officially and requests the States concerned to give these subjects the highest priority; he proposes a draft resolution on this subject.

After a discussion, the Committee adopts the following resolution:

The International Committee of Legal Metrology

CONSIDERING that almost all measuring instruments are being equipped with electronic devices,

CONSIDERING the importance of a common OIML International Document being available as a catalogue for Reporting Secretariats in order to choose the appropriate environmental and other tests to be included in the relevant International Recommendations,

CONGRATULATES the SP2-Sr6 and SP7-Sr2 Secretariats on the progress achieved in the drafting of requirements for electronic measuring instruments in general and in the application of these requirements to electronic weighing instruments,

REQUESTS the SP2-Sr6 and SP7-Sr2 Secretariats to do their utmost in order to finish the drafting of the respective texts during their next meetings in 1985.

## 7.8. Recalibration intervals of measurement standards and standard devices

Mr ATHANE states that the two documents prepared by the ILAC task Force E were taken into consideration by OIML.

One was adopted as International Document No 10 and should be published before the meeting of ILAC 84. The second will be published by BIML as « examples of recalibration intervals ].

As eventual future action it would be appropriate to examine the possibility of creating a new Reporting Secretariat.

## CLOSURE OF THE 20th COMMITTEE MEETING

Mr. BIRKELAND informs his colleagues of the imminent retirement of Mr PERLSTAIN, who is unanimously nominated as Honorary Member.

Mr PERLSTAIN recalls with pleasure that he has worked with OIML since 1957. He underlines the pragmatic and rational character of the Organization, right at the start, which allowed it to achieve a lot with rather restricted resources.

He is disturbed by the burocratic tendencies which became evident during the last few years but is confident that the Committee will be able to keep the OIML activities within reasonable limits, so that the International Recommendations will always be clear and concise, and ready for direct use in national regulations. He thanks all his colleagues in the Committee and hopes that he will meet them from time to time as Honorary Member.

Mr BIRKELAND closes the meeting thanking once again all the participants, the interpreters and especially Mrs LAHTEENMÄKI for the most remarkable organization which she herself and her collaborators have accomplished, thus ensuring the sucess of the Committee meeting and of the Conference.
