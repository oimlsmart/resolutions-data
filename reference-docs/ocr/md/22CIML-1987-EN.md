# OPENING ADDRESS by K. BIRKELAND, President of the Committee

</div>

Ladies and Gentlemen,

It gives me great pleasure to welcome you, happily a large number, to the twenty second meeting of our Committee, meeting as agreed eighteen months ago in April 1986 when we commemorated, in this same room, OIML's first thirty years.

It is good to look back on the past from time to time to remind ourselves of the deeds of our predecessors and, as sometimes happens, to receive inspiration which enables us to prepare and build a better future.

Today we shall not be looking back but, on the contrary, we shall be looking to the future, to the next four or five years. This twenty-second meeting of the Committee will actually be mainly devoted to preparations for the Eighth International Conference of Legal Metrology which, in October 1988, is required to lay down the broad lines of OIML's activities for the next four-year period and provide our Organization, its working groups and its Bureau with the people, money and equipment needed to make this programme of work a reality.

But before coming to these future matters I would like, as is usual, to make a rapid survey of the events which have been prominent in the life of OIML since our last meeting.

First of all it was with great sadness that we learned of the death at the beginning of this year of our greatly esteemed colleague from Monaco, Alain VATRICAN, Secretary General of the Scientific Centre of Monaco and Member of our International Committee since May, 1973.

Alain VATRICAN had participated actively in our meetings throughout those fourteen years, as well as in some of our working groups, particularly those on the measurement of pollution. It is a field in which the scientists of the Principality of Monaco have always taken a special interest.

I ask you to observe a moment's silence in his memory.

Thank you.

Let me welcome Mr VEGLIA, who represents the Principality of Monaco.

There have been other changes in the composition of our Committee.

Our former Vice-President, Walter MUHE, announced last year both his approaching retirement and the name of his successor on the Committee, Dr KOCHSIEK, who now represents the Federal Republic of Germany.

Other changes have occurred ;

in Australia, where Mr BIRCH has replaced Mr PETRY,

in Brazil, where Mr MASAO ITO has replaced Mr TAVORA VEADO,

in the Republic of Korea, where Mr SON BOCK-GILL has replaced Mr CHANG KI-JUNG,

in the Popular Democratic Republic of Korea, where Mr DJEUNG KI TCHEUL has replaced Mr TCHEU HWA TCHOUN,

in Egypt, where Mr SALEM has replaced Mr SOBHY,

in Guinea, where Mr CONDE has retired ; this country is represented by Messrs IBRAHIMA CAMARA and TANOU DIALLO,

in Japan, where Mr HATTORI has replaced Mr IIZUKA,

in the Netherlands, where Mr FABER has replaced Mr NIEUWLAND.

I have just received a letter from Mr HILL, President of the Office for Standards and Measurements in Czechoslovakia, informing me that he had to relinquish his duties in our Committee and that his country is from now on represented by Mr CIBAK.

In addition, at the beginning of the year, we have had the pleasure of welcoming a new Member State, the Portugal, which is represented by Mr CRUZ.

Finally, our Bureau has also undergone some changes in that Mr TURSKI has retired and been replaced by Dr EMERSON, who worked prior to his arrival here at the National Engineering Laboratory of the United Kingdom.

To all the new Members of the Committee and to the new member of the staff of the Bureau I am happy to voice again my welcome and sincere good wishes.

Let us turn now to the purpose of our meeting. As I mentioned earlier, it is mainly to begin preparing for our Eighth Conference, now only a year away.

Of course the Committee will have to deal with all matters of interest in the life of our Organization and it is not my intention to list them all in these opening remarks. Some, such as activities of relevance to developing countries, or liaison with other international institutions, will be studied in the course of our discussions on Thursday or Friday.

I would particularly like to draw your attention to three items. First, the technical activities of our Pilot Secretariats and our Reporting Secretariats, activities which are still the main raison d'être of OIML.

Three years ago we introduced a planning process into these activities, leading to the development of work programmes covering four-year periods. Although it is still too early to be able to draw any final conclusions about the good and bad effects of this planning exercise, we can now try to evaluate some of them with the hope of improving the process for the next planning period, which begins in 1989 and ends in 1992. Item 5 of our agenda will be entirely devoted to this study, which must be closely linked with item 6 on the activities of the various Secretariats.

An increase in the efficiency of our technical working groups and a more profitable use of the limited resources of our Members States' metrological services are, of course, ends in themselves. But we must not forget that any increase in the activities of our Pilot Secretariats or Reporting Secretariats has, in the short term, a direct effect on the situation at the Bureau. The latter has not benefitted from any increase in the number of its staff since 1973; and even if improved equipment has enabled it to meet an enormous increase in its responsibilities and tasks, we are bound to acknowledge that a point of saturation has been reached such that there is a risk of its becoming a bottleneck if the Bureau is granted no additional resources.

Our role during this meeting will be to study the situation and do our best to advise the Bureau as to how it should approach the Conference, with the best hope of success, in asking for the increase in staff and funds which it needs to meet the responsibilities which we entrust to it.

The third point which, in concluding, I should like to raise is, as you may have guessed, that of certification.

The creation of an OIML system of certification was not foreseen as one of the objectives listed in the Convention constituting OIML.

During the seventies it was, nevertheless, apparent that that represented an important goal, given the way in which our society has evolved, the increase in international trade, the need to improve product quality, and especially the extraordinary

diversity and complexity of these products. Their systematic control, using classical procedures applied by national certification organizations working independently of each other, is becoming an insurmountable task.

That is true in all technical fields, not only in metrology, and numerous international institutions are working, like OIML, to resolve these problems by trying to set up international certification systems which significantly diminish the load on national organizations.

Twelve years ago OIML became something of a pioneer in starting work on such a system. Perhaps we embarked on it too soon ? Anyway, our Committee concluded two years ago that the opportune moment to create the OIML Mark had not yet arrived.

However, events move along quickly, and today I wonder whether it might not be appropriate to reconsider the question. It is, I believe, the key to the future of our Organization, and it is my intention to devote whatever time is needed to it so that our discussions may, if it seems appropriate, result in new actions being placed on the Presidential Council and the Secretariat SP2-Sr4.

There Ladies, Gentlemen, dear Colleagues, are the main guidelines that I would like to give you, it you agree, for the next three days of meetings.

Before we get down to the real discussions I now call on the Director of the Bureau to call the roll of delegates and to give us any information he may have for us about the conduct of the meeting.

## 3.2. The financial position in 1987 and 1988

Mr ATHANE reported that at the end of 1986 and the beginning of 1987 OIML had been in a difficult financial position because seventeen Member States had not paid their subscriptions for 1986, so that it had had to make extensive use of its reserve fund to meet its expenses and had had to restrict the latter as much as possible to the bare essentials. In the course of 1987 the position had improved, a large part of the arrears having been paid and also many States having paid their subscriptions for 1987 promptly.

As for the expenses in 1987, the forecasts made at the end of 1986 seemed still to be correct, mainly due to a rate of inflation which had remained close to the predicted rate.

Mr ATHANE added that if there was no deterioration in the financial position between then and the end of 1987 the Bureau would proceed with the purchase of word-processing equipment, which was urgently needed. This expense, which was of an exceptional nature, would amount to about 150000 French francs.

In 1988, Mr ATHANE said, the Member States' subscriptions would go up by only 4.5 per cent in relation to those of 1987, by the application of the decision of the Seventh Conference to reduce the budget to take account of the fall in the rate of inflation. Thus the sum of the contributory shares for 1988 would be 780000 French francs less than the maximum sum voted by the Conference.

In reply to a question put by Mr PETERSEN, Mr ATHANE described the means adopted by the Bureau, using diplomatic channels and Members of the Committee as intermediaries, to remind Member States in arrears that they must pay their subscriptions with minimum delay.

Mr TUKAI suggested that reminders should be sent more frequently.

Messrs CLAPHAM, FANNING, KNAPP and PETERSEN emphasised that a word processor was an absolute necessity for the Bureau, given the amount of work that it had to do. They urged the Bureau to make this purchase as a matter of urgency, as soon as it became evident that a large part of the subscriptions for 1988 would be paid, without even waiting for their actual payment.

## 5. WORK PLANNING

## 5.1. Planning exercise for the period 1985 to 1988

M. PETIK drew the attention of the Committee to the final drafts of the programmes of work that the Bureau had presented, in a uniform format, based on the draft work programmes drawn up by the Secretariats in 1985 and approved by the Committee in 1986. Although it was not then possible to draw any final conclusions it could be said that, of 150 Reporting Secretariats, 40 were inactive (their work being either finished or not yet started). Of the 110 active Secretariats, three quarters were proceeding according to plan and a quarter had slipped behind. Finally Mr PETIK emphasised that, in his view, the work plans should be periodically brought up to date because, on the one hand, the work evolved quickly and, on the other, changes in countries' economic conditions could cause changes in the resources which the Member States were able to devote to OIML, as well as in the level of interest which Member States might have for any particular work. Another reason for periodic revision was that it was difficult to predict how many successive pre-drafts would be needed before agreement was reached among the collaborating countries.

In reply to a question from Mr OHLON, Mr PETIK said that it was impossible to specify particular areas of activity which were noticeably ahead of plan or were behind.

In response to a question from Mr PETERSEN, Mr ATHANE indicated that, out of 35 or 40 drafts which in 1985, could conceivably be submitted to the Eighth Conference for sanction, 20 to 25 were sufficiently advanced and would be submitted if the results of the postal ballots were favourable.

## 5.2. Preparation of the plan for the period 1989 to 1992

Mr BIRKELAND reminded the Committee of the reasons which had led it to decide to introduce a planning system for the work of OIML, and to instruct the Presidential Council to prepare the system and to put it into effect. The second planning period, for the years 1989 to 1992, should by then be entering its planning phase, even if the results of the first period were far from being known in their entirety. Experience had shown that the problem of setting priority criteria, that is the importance to be attached to each work topic, had not been completely resolved, and that this question needed to be studied in depth before constructing the plan for the second period. For that purpose the Bureau had submitted proposals which stemmed from ideas and comments put forward by some Committee Members.

Mr BERTRAN was convinced of the need to set priorities, because plans could not be made in a vacuum, without being based on some priority criteria. He feared, nevertheless, that the list of eleven criteria submitted by the Bureau was too extensive: too many priorities meant no priorities! He proposed that the Committee limit itself to five or six priorities, as proposed by the Netherlands, and that it should select, not priorities, but criteria. Each work topic could be examined to see which criteria were satisfied (for example: a request from another institution, elimination of technical barriers and the revision of an outdated Recommendation, satisfy simultaneously three criteria). The topics which satisfy simultaneously the largest number of criteria should have the highest priority.

Mr CLAPHAM expressed his general support for Mr BERTRAN's remarks. He added that it should not be forgotten that the aim of OIML, deriving from the Convention, was the unification of regulations and methods of control in the field of legal metrology. Criteria numbers 1 and 3 in the list proposed by the Bureau were entirely relevant to the aim of unification. But other criteria, such as numbers 5, 6 and 7, though of great importance from a technical and economic viewpoint, did not have the same degree of importance for OIML, for they were not part of legal metrology, or at least in many countries such as the United Kingdom.

M. FABER also thought that OIML should limit its range of activities by deciding upon priorities. OIML had been much more active in recent years and could not continue to do more and more without the risk of compromising the efficiency of the Organization. The government of the Netherlands was very anxious not to see the work of OIML, and hence the demands on money and staff, increase beyond that which was strictly needed. For that it was imperative, difficult though the task might be, to succeed in establishing work priorities and then observing them.

Messrs PETERSEN and OHLON gave their views on the list of priorities submitted by the Bureau, particularly with regard to the regrouping of some of the criteria.

Mr OHLON added that the criteria submitted by the Bureau were of different kinds and that it was difficult to put them side by side or to compare them. He supported Mr CLAPHAM in the belief that criteria numbers 1, 2 and 10 were of the greatest importance, given the objectives of OIML as determined by the Convention. Sweden, likewise, did not consider criteria 5, 6 and 7 to be in the field of legal metrology.

Mr BIRKELAND remarked that the Committee should not discuss the priorities by which it would decide what topics should be included in OIML's work programme without keeping in mind the fact that other factors weigh in these decisions ; in particular the number of States agreeing to participate in the work.

Up to then five P-Members had been enough to create a Reporting Secretariat. For the future it would no doubt be necessary to increase that number ; but in fact the greater the priority attached to a work topic, the greater are the number of States wishing to participate in the work.

Mr FANNING was in full agreement with that idea, though he feared that there would be difficulties in settling on too high a figure for the minimum number of P-Members. It was to be hoped that the revision of former Recommendations would feature among the priority criteria to be retained.

Mr FABER explained how the Netherlands had chosen to retain only some of the criteria proposed by the Bureau: requests from other international institutions, problems arising from new technologies applied to measuring instruments used in trade, the needs of health and security, all appeared to be of high priority and ought to receive the maximum of attention from the Secretariats of OIML, to the detriment of other topics of much lower priority.

Mr OHLON, referring again to the objectives of the Organization laid down in the Convention, repeated that items 1 and 3 of the list submitted by the Bureau ought to receive the highest priority. And particularly as regards item 1, special attention should be given, he said, to cooperation between OIML and the European organizations concerned with metrology and standardization. He thought, on the other hand, that care should be taken not to give priority to subjects already dealt with by other institutions such as ISO, IEC, WHO, etc.

Mr CLAPHAM strongly supported that view. He thought that, as well as positive criteria, there could be negative criteria, that is criteria of low priority. The fact that a subject was already dealt with by ISO or IEC would be one such criterion of low priority. Certainly one would have to be careful and not delete from the OIML programme all subjects treated by another institution. It could happen that it was that other institution that was encroaching on OIML's territory; and it might also be desirable that two institutions, one of which was OIML, should work simultaneously on the subject from different points of view.

Mr EDGERLY expressed his complete agreement with what Mr CLAPHAM had said. He also thought that Mr BERTRAN was absolutely right in making a distinction between " criteria 》 and " priorities ]. in the real sense. He thought that the list submitted by the Bureau should be revised and arranged more logically. For example, criteria numbers 5, 6 and 7 were only sub-sets of criterion number 4. Criterion number 8 was just one particular example of criterion number 2. Thus the list could be reduced to a more reasonable number of criteria and the latter could then be used more easily by the Pilot Secretariats in planning their work programmes (with the additional need, as Mr CLAPHAM had said, to avoid unnecessary duplication of the work of other institutions).

At Mr PETERSEN's request, Mr BIRKELAND said that the list submitted by the Bureau did not make proposals for new work for which new Pilot Secretariats should be set up, but criteria by which existing Secretariats could decide whether or not a topic should be included in the draft work programmes which they would submit to the Committee for acceptance.

Mr EMERSON remarked that the eleven criteria could be divided into two groups : numbers 1, 2, 3, 9, 10 and 11 constituted motivations for working on a given subject, while numbers 4, 5, 6, 7 and 8 referred to particular technical fields. The Committee could confine its discussion to the first group ; it was not really possible to discuss motivations and technical fields at the same time.

Mr BIRKELAND expressed satisfaction with the debate which had just taken place. He thought, nevertheless, that it was not possible to arrive immediately at any definite conclusions. It was no doubt necessary to go on thinking about this

fundamental problem and he invited his colleagues to send in any additional views that they might have to the Bureau, after the meeting. He thought that the Bureau could then study all the opinions that had been expressed during the meeting, or afterwards by correspondence, and submit this compilation to the Presidential Council meeting in January 1988. By this means concrete proposals could no doubt be submitted to the Committee in good time for the twenty-third meeting, during which the work programmes for 1989 to 1992 would be adopted.

Mr ATHANE submitted some proposals on the manner in which the Bureau could advance the study of the subject, especially to try to classify the criteria in relation to each other.

Mr KOCH thought that it might well be interesting to try to classify the criteria by importance but, in any case, the question of available means would arise. It was no use considering a subject to be very important if a working group could not be found to take it on.

Following the discussion the Committee adopted the following resolution :

The International Committee of Legal Metrology,

HAVING TAKEN NOTE of the comments, submitted by certain Member States, on the preparations for the planning period 1989 to 1992,

HAVING TAKEN NOTE of the proposals of the Bureau,

HAVING HEARD the oral comments of Committee Members,

NOTING that planning criteria are required so that the plan for 1989 to 1992 may be prepared in time,

INVITES Committee Members to inform the Bureau of their views on planning criteria and priorities and on the criteria for the inclusion of a subject in the work programme,

INSTRUCTS the Bureau to compile the information and views of Committee Members on this subject and to bring them to the attention of the Presidential Council.

CHARGES the Presidential Council with considering and weighing the views expressed and making a recommendation to the Twenty-third meeting of the Committee,

INSTRUCTS the Bureau to proceed with the other aspects of the planning exercise for the period 1989 to 1992 in accordance with the proposals of BIML presented at the meeting.

## 6. WORK OF THE SECRETARIATS

## 6.1. State of progress of work

Mr BIRKELAND introduced the paper, "State of progress of the work of the Secretariats of OIML ", of which an updated edition had been distributed to all participants. The paper aroused no comment.

## 6.2. Particular Secretariats (creation, abolition, assignment of responsibilities)

Mr ATHANE gave an account of the situation in which some of the Secretariats found themselves and with regard to which the Committee made the following decisions :

- SP 2-Sr 3: « Legal qualification of measuring instruments »: this Secretariat was abolished at the request of SP 2, for its activities are entirely covered by SP 22. The revision of DI 3, if needed, will be undertaken by SP 22 in liaison with the Bureau.

## 6.3. Working method of OIML Secretariats

Mr BIRKELAND pointed out that the document Working Method of OIML Secretariats was now some years old and should therefore be well known and

correctly applied by Secretariats. It had to be said, however, that on certain occasions, and particularly at meetings, this working method had not always been correctly put into practice and that some participants were completely unaware of its existence. It was worth considering, therefore, how knowledge and application of this document could be improved. It would also be appropriate to improve and complete the paper in the light of the experience which had been gained and perhaps to take account of the new procedures arising from the planning process. Such a republication would, in any case, have the advantage of drawing attention to the document, particularly that of the new experts who had not been actively participating two or three years earlier, when the preceding edition had been published.

Mr BIRKELAND concluded his introduction in stressing the importance of the document, not only for the internal working of OIML, but also as an informative document on the activities of OIML which was useful to all the international institutions which cooperated with OIML or who were concerned with metrology.

Mr ATHANE thought that one of the reasons why the Working Method was less well observed was that the Bureau had not, in the past two years, been able to take part in meetings of Secretariats as much as it had done formerly, for two reasons :

- the increased work load which prevented staff of the Bureau having enough time to make the journeys needed to participate,

- the Bureau's financial difficulties, which had meant that the Director had to manage expenditure very carefully and to restrict travel expenses, which were already very high, as much as possible.

Mr ATHANE also thought that it would be opportune to produce a brief summary of the Working Method, focused on the holding of meetings. The summary could be made available to all organizers of OIML meetings so that the participants might be made aware of the main working rules.

Finally, Mr ATHANE recognized that during the last twelve to eighteen months some aspects of the Working Method, particularly those concerning time scales, had not always been observed for it had sometimes been necessary to speed things up to make sure that drafts would be ready for the Conference.

Mr PETERSEN thought that the Working Method was good as it was. In fact, what could be criticised were the excessive differences that sometimes existed between the ways of working of this or that Secretariat; it was more the application of the Working Method, rather than the Method itself, that needed to be reconsidered.

Mr EDGERLY was of the same opinion. It was not a question of an inadequate Working Method, but of experts in need of education. He also approved of the idea of preparing a summary giving the essential working rules, particularly those for the conduct of meetings: at every meeting the chairman should devote fifteen or twenty minutes to reviewing this summary and make sure that all the participants were well aware of the Working Method. Perhaps some improvements could be made, but no doubt they would be marginal. Mr EDGERLY concluded by saying that it was an important responsibility of every Member of the Committee to ensure that the national experts who organised or chaired meetings, or who supported or participated in the work of the Secretariat, were fully conversant with the Working Method.

Mr KOCH asked that people organising OIML meetings pay more attention to the difficulties of travel : times of flights, fares etc. For example, if a meeting started at noon, participants from neighbouring countries could travel to it in the morning and thereby save the expense of a night in a hotel, provided that the venue was near enough to the airport. Having to travel at the weekend had some disadvantages, but sometimes offered the advantage of reduced air fares. Advice on these matters could be given in the Working Method.

Mr BIRCH thought that the organisation and planning of meetings was a matter that should be re-examined. For countries far removed from Europe, such as Australia, an expert could not spend twenty four hours in an aeroplane to attend a meeting lasting two days, and then return home. The lack of coordination between dates and venues of meetings was a great hindrance to Australia's participation and that of other countries in the work of OIML. The importance attached to speeding up the work to increase the number of Recommendations, as cited by the Director of the Bureau, was certainly understandable, but a much more important factor was that the Recommendations be unanimously accepted and applied by the Member States; and for that it was essential that all the Member States be able to participate effectively in the work, without being hindered by problems of time limits, travel, etc. The participants in the Eighth Conference would be able to judge for themselves what was entailed in a journey between Europe and Australia, and they would then realise the difficulties faced by remote countries in participating in the work of the Secretariats.

Mr BIRCH concluded by asking that a recommendation be included in the Working Method whereby several OIML meetings should be organised at certain times of the year at the same or neighbouring places. That would help not only the remote countries to participate, but also all the others, for they would even be able to plan their experts' travel.

Mr BIRKELAND recognised that Mr BIRCH's remarks were well founded and observed that the Secretariats were beginning to make an effort to coordinate their meetings. He also drew attention to the possibility of taking part in the work of OIML by correspondence.

Mr THULIN observed that countries which could not attend meetings sometimes failed to take advantage of the opportunity that was given to take part in the work by correspondence; they did not make their views known when the text was still at the preliminary-draft stage, and it was not until the final stage had been reached that they reacted and made known their views, by which time it was often too late to take them into account.

Mr KOCH thought that the development of Telefax would enable an exchange of views with countries who had not sent representatives to take place while a meeting as going on.

The International Committee of Legal Metrology,

HAVING HEARD the oral comments of Committee Members,

RECOGNISING that the provisions contained in the « Working Method of OIML Secretariats » are in general entirely appropriate,

CONSIDERING nevertheless that some points of detail could be completed or improved,

CONSIDERING that the problems that arise are almost always due to incorrect implementation of these provisions, mainly due to their being poorly known,

## 7. CERTIFICATION

Mr BIRKELAND introduced this item by stressing the importance for OIML of the question of mutual recognition of metrological controls and, more generally, of what was meant by international certification. The internationalization of the exchange of merchandise, goods and services had increased significantly in all fields, including that of metrology. At the same time, national metrological services had often seen their resources diminishing or stagnating at their present level, or at any rate not increasing as rapidly as the volume of work. A way of resolving some of the problems encountered at local level lay, no doubt, in the solution of the problems of international certification, thanks to better rationalization of activity.

Mr BIRKELAND also drew attention to the fact that many regional and international organizations were engaged in certification, and be added that OIML too had a role to play. He cited all the work done on this subject by OIML during the previous dozen or so years, before asking the Bureau to indicate the points which the Committee needed to discuss.

Mr PETIK began his introduction with a brief account of the studies already completed and of the decisions taken by the Conference and the Committee. On his suggestion the application of the resolutions adopted by the Committee in April 1986 was studied, one by one.

## a) Bilateral or multilateral agreements

Mr PETIK reminded Committee Members that they had been invited to send to the Bureau, if they could and thought it useful, details of any bilateral or multilateral agreements in which their countries were participants. The Bureau had summarised the information. Only agreements dealing strictly with legal metrology were included, not general agreements like those on accreditation.

Mr CLAPHAM thought that this exclusion of general agreements was dangerous. Already, and increasingly in the future, agreements on metrological matters were being and would be caught up in more general agreements on questions of the accreditation of test laboratories.

Mr PETIK said that he was well aware of that but that he would have to try, nevertheless, to draw a dividing line between the agreements which were relevant to or which included legal metrology, and those which did not concern it. The Bureau would see to it that this demarcation was as nearly as possible correctly done, and the general agreements which included metrology would be systematically catalogued.

Mr BIRKELAND drew attention to an aspect of the agreement concluded between the EFTA countries which he thought was important : of the five heads of the agreement, the fifth related to the fact that OIML Recommendations should, to every possible extent, be used as the basis for reciprocal recognition. In his view it was very important that the Members of CIML introduce such a reference to OIML in the bilateral or multilateral agreements that are signed by their countries.

Finally some countries, among them Hungary, informed the Committee of agreements recently concluded.

## b) Exchanges of information on the results of pattern approval evaluations

Mr PETIK gave an account of the information on this subject that had been received by the Bureau.

Mr FABER gave some insight into the very flexible and informal way in which the Netherlands sought information on pattern approval, evaluations already completed in other countries.

Mr ISSAEV proposed that very brief announcements about the various pattern approvals given by Member States be published in the Bulletin.

Mr ATHANE, replying to that proposal, said that, interesting though it was, there were very great difficulties in the way : the number of approvals, the translation into French or English, or both, of pattern approvals given in other languages, the difficulty of identifying with absolute certainty the pattern concerned, etc.

Mr KOCH reported that Switzerland envisaged supplying the manufacturer, first on demand, but later systematically, with declarations of conformity which the manufacturer could then present to the metrological services of another country. There was the current difficulty that the legal requirements in force were not completely identical to those of OIML, but they were being brought into line.

c) Declarations of conformity of an instrument or of a series of instruments

Mr PETIK said that the Bureau had not received any reports on the use being made of the « declaration of conformity » recommended by the Committee.

Mr PETERSEN said that Denmark was setting up a system for issuing such certificates, for they were of great interest to the manufacturers who had already approached the Danish Council of Metrology on that subject to obtain details. The manufacturers had also asked whether these certificates could also extend to ISO or IEC. Mr PETERSEN said that his reply was always that these certificates were limited strictly to the requirements of legal metrology as laid down by OIML, without, however, restricting them to fields of metrology legal in Denmark. For Danish manufacturers the chief attraction of these certificates was in marketing and exporting, to prove conformity with OIML.

In Mr PETERSEN's view such certificates could, moreover, open the door to a more elaborate system of certification, including mutual recognition of tests.

Mr PETERSEN added that in the immediate future, the individual certificates issued in Denmark would be from specially authorised laboratories under the supervision of the Danish authorities. The system of individual certificates would exist in parallel with the system for pattern approval, and would be mainly an aid to export ; the certificates would therefore be issued on the demand of the manufacturer, on whom would continue to rest the responsibility for conformity to the requirements. Finally, the form and content of the certificates would have to be strictly defined : for example, the use of the OIML logo ; or again, details of the methods of measurements used by the authorised laboratory, etc.

Mr THULIN reminded the Committee that originally its decision to institute a declaration of conformity was founded on the needs of the developing countries who imported measuring instruments ; the metrological services of these countries could thus be certain that the instruments imported, identified in the declaration by their numbers, had already been examined by a service of an industrialised country and found to conform to OIML.

Mr KOCH thought that, for the mass export of instruments, a declaration of conformity was inappropriate and that an OIML mark would be preferable.

Mr OHLON observed, first that the terminology used by OIML on this subject (declaration or attestation of conformity) was not in conformity with that of ISO Guide No. 2. He then indicated that nothing prevented the issue of such a certificate in Sweden, but the main difficulty which presented itself was the lack of harmonization with regard to the requirements for electronic devices.

Mr PETERSEN added to his previous remarks by saying that in his opinion it would be useful to publicise the system so that the manufacturers knew of its existence and were in a position to ask the national authorities to issue such certificates.

## d) Mention of conformity to the requirements of OIML in pattern-approval decisions

Mr PETIK reminded the Committee that at its meeting in April 1986 it had looked at the possibility of recommending that Member States include in their national pattern approvals a note to the effect, if such was the case, that the pattern concerned conformed, in whole or in part, to the requirements of the relevant OIML Recommendations. That proposal was not approved by the Committee (see minutes of the Twenty-first Meeting of CIML, point 13.1). In the meantime the Bureau had concluded that the original idea could be taken up again in another form and had suggested to the Presidential Council that the matter be reexamined. At the beginning of 1987 Mr BIRKELAND had put before the Council a draft declaration of conformity applicable to a pattern of a measuring instrument (and not to a well identified instrument or series of instruments, as in c above). This draft was well received when sent to the Members of the Committee, though only twelve Members replied.

Mr SEILER said that the PTB was taking a first step in informing manufacturers in the Federal Republic of Germany that such declarations of conformity to the requirements of OIML Recommendations could be supplied where the said Recommendations were sufficiently complete to enable the pattern of the instrument and its characteristics to be correctly specified. The form developed by the Nordic countries had been used as a model. Mr SEILER added that the hoped for a favourable reaction from the manufacturers, and that other countries would follow this initiative, which was a necessary stage towards the establishment of a more complete and all-encompassing system of certification.

Mrs LAHTEENMAKI said that there had been much thought in her country about the administrative and technical problems arising from the possible introduction of declarations of conformity. It had become apparent that if it were introduced the field would be clear for an overall OIML system of certification, and so this proposal was considered to be entirely acceptable.

Mr KOCH considered that the issue of declarations of conformity constituted a first step; a second step would be the recognition of those certificates. This recognition could be done very flexibly, a country being able to decide to recognise the declarations of this or that country, for this or that type of instrument.

Mr OHLON thought that such certificates would be particularly useful if they were accompanied by the results of tests, as proposed by Austria. Moreover OIML should take inspiration from what was being done in other fields, especially in electronics, where schemes of mutual recognition were already in existence.

Mr BERTRAN, continuing Mr OHLON's line of thought, added that these declarations would have to make a distinction between information and approval. A manufacturer should not make those of his customers who were ill-acquainted with national metrological requirements think that an instrument endowed with an OIML declaration of conformity was legally acceptable in any country.

Mr BIRKELAND expressed his approval of what had just been said. Originally the declaration of conformity was suppose to be issued by one metrological service at the request of another. But what Mr SEILER had described would be a document issued by a service but used by the manufacturer concerned. The way in which the document was drawn up should clearly show its limits of applicability, so that no one might be tempted to misuse it.

Mr PETERSEN considered that the use of these declarations of conformity would be greatly eased if a revised model form, with notes on its use, were devised by the Bureau.

Mr KOCH also thought that it would be wise to publish a list of Recommendations with respect to which these declarations might be made.

Mr SEILER undertook to send information about PTB's experience in this matter to the Bureau as soon as possible.

Mr EMERSON remarked that declarations of conformity could be supplied for instruments in a given country without regard to whether or not the instruments were subject to legal metrological control in that country. In the long run that could induce users to buy only those instruments which were the subject of such declarations and so bring pressure on their administrations to bring in regulations which were in conformity with OIML.

Mr BIRKELAND closed the discussion by asking the Bureau to review the draft declaration of conformity, to amend it and if necessary complete it, to consider which Recommendations could be cited in declarations of conformity, and for what types of instruments, and to inform the Council of the results. Then a developed proposal could be sent to Committee Members with the suggestion that they contemplate issuing certificates of conformity on an entirely voluntary basis.

## e) Future activities with regard to certification

Mr BIRKELAND reminded the Committee that in 1983 it had examined the proposals from an ad-hoc group and has concluded (1) that it could not at that time envisage the creation of an OIML system of certification. The Committee had asked the Bureau to preserve the files relating to this work for possible later use and to carry out through SP2-Sr4 some specific actions to resolve particular problems and ease the future establishment of an OIML system of certification.

Mr BIRKELAND observed that there had been a rapid evolution of the situation with the development of regional certification (for example within the European Economic Community), bilateral agreements, the efforts of ISO, IEC, ILAC, etc. It could be asked whether the time had not come to reconsider the question and perhaps to begin work again on the creation of an OIML system of certification.

Mr ISSAEV drew his colleagues' attention to a written comment from the USSR in the Bureau's paper : the basis of the future OIML system of certification could only be constituted by a set of Recommendations laying down specifications, verification procedures and test methods applicable to the instruments concerned. Since a complete set of OIML Recommendations did not exist, a certification system could not be established.

Mr KOCH was in general agreement with Mr ISSAEV. He observed, however, that the various laboratories or services in various countries were not identically equipped and doubtless it would be difficult for them to carry out all the standard procedures for the checking of instruments. Mr KOCH thought that in the first place Recommendations were needed which stipulated the requirements applicable to the instruments proper, and that harmonization of the equipment and methods of control would come later.

Mr ATHANE thought it useful to draw attention to a point which was made in a paper submitted by Finland : if, as envisaged in the Convention, national regulations were based on the OIML Recommendations, national pattern approvals would automatically constitute recognition of conformity to OIML. The establishment of the certification system would certainly thus be made easier.

Mr OHLON expressed his support for what had just been said and thought that national regulations ought to be identical to the Recommendations and not just based on them. It was also important to harmonise the means by which conformity

to the requirements was proven. Finally it was extremely important to create a climate of mutual confidence between the people whose task it was to do the tests. In fact a sufficiently high level of reproducibility between laboratories was needed, and that implied at the same time the use of uniform test procedures and adequate competence on the part of the various laboratories. The Nordic experience showed how profitable was cooperation at the practical level between the people who effectively were expected to do the tests.

As regarded the way in which the OIML system of certification, properly speaking, would work, Mr OHLON proposed the introduction of an OIML Mark . However, this OIML Mark was not to be confused with the logo used by the Bureau on its letter head and the documents it published. A distinction would also have to be made between Mark and Certification System and for that reference should be made to ISO Guide N $ ^{\circ} $ 2. In effect, OIML would never be the certifying body , able itself to direct a certification system, as defined by ISO. On the other hand the OIML Mark could be attributed the following significance : tests had been done which proved that the product satisfied the requirements of OIML ; this mark might or might not, moreover, be supplemented by a national mark.

Mr KOCH indicated that in Switzerland preparations were in hand to accept, for certain instruments, initial verifications done by certain other countries (with, on the part of the Swiss authorities, second degree control by sampling). However, it would be difficult legally in Switzerland to accept the official marks of other countries. By contrast, there would be fewer problems in accepting the marks of an international character.

Mr BERTRAN raised a number of problems which, in his view, would have to be resolved before trying to set up a certification system ;

- the system might comprise certification of patterns of instruments, certification of individual instruments, or both;

- was the creation of a system compatible with the Convention by which OIML was constituted?

- was the creation of a system compatible with Member States' legislation?

He proposed that the Bureau study these questions before all else.

Mr CLAPHAM, in expressing his support for the last remarks, said that, for example, it was not possible in the United Kingdom to accept an instrument bearing the OIML mark, for the regulations concerning the meanings of marks were very strict. There would also have to be protection against the fraudulent use of the mark, for example by a manufacturer who himself placed the mark on the instruments he produced, whether dishonestly or in good faith, because he thought that the instruments satisfied OIML. Finally there would have to be some assurance that the national authorities themselves knew what they were doing in applying the mark, and could therefore be relied on.

Mr KOCH did not want to criticise the rather hesitant views that had been expressed, but nevertheless it was necessary that OIML should have a mark of conformity, and have it quickly. That mark would be purely informative in nature, not binding. Countries could, nevertheless, and purely voluntarily, recognise the mark as equivalent to the national marks in particular instances, for example where there was a bilateral agreement.

Mrs LAHTEENMAKI agreed with Mr KOCH's view and pointed out that there had been discussions within EFTA which had led to similar conclusions in support of the establishment of an OIML mark.

Mr BIRKELAND summarised the views that had been expressed and suggested a programme that the Bureau could follow to pursue work on this topic.

In conclusion the Committee adopted the following resolution :

The International Committee of Legal Metrology,

HAVING HEARD the oral comments made by some of its Members on the appropriateness of establishing an OIML system of certification or an OIML mark,

RECALLING the studies already completed on this subject by an ad hoc group in the years 1975 to 1982,

CONCLUDES that the time has come to restart work on the subject,

INSTRUCTS the Bureau to make the analyses, compilations and enquiries it deems appropriate,

CHARGES the Presidential Council with the supervision of this work and the submission of a progress report to the next meeting of the Committee.

## 10. CATEGORIES OF OIML PUBLICATIONS

Mr EDGERLY reminded the Committee that the Presidential Council had given him the task of producing a proposal whose object would be to make official the publication by the Bureau of brochures which fall neither into the category of International Recommendations nor into that of International Documents. These brochures could come originally from the Bureau itself or from a Pilot Secretariat or Reporting Secretariat (and in the last case would have been approved by the International Working Group of the Secretariat), or from other sources. They would be examined by the Bureau, and the decision whether or not to publish them would be the Director's. These brochures could cover very different fields : information manuals for developing countries, compilations of existing Recommendations, reports, etc.

Mr THULIN pointed out that some years earlier the Bureau has produced some brochures which were meant particularly for developing countries. It had quickly become apparent that some of them were of much wider interest, and other brochures had been produced for the use of all Member States and Corresponding States. However, the distribution of these brochures had been confined, as before, to those states; they had not been sent to private users (manufacturers, private laboratories...) except in exceptional circumstances. The Bureau had nevertheless reconsidered this question; and the sale of these brochures, in the same manner as International Recommendations and Documents, was envisaged.

In reply to a question from Mr FABER the respective roles of the Pilot or Reporting Secretariats and the Bureau were clarified as regards brochures prepared by a Secretariat.

Mr BIRKELAND closed the discussion in concluding that the Committee accepted this proposal. He left to the Bureau the task of resolving questions of detail such as designations, possible numbering systems, etc. for these brochures.

## 11. OIML SEMINARS

Mr THULIN spoke of the seminar on the measurement of large quantities of liquids, which the Bureau had organised at Arles, in the south of France, in April 1987, with the support of the French metrological authorities and, as regarded the visits and technical demonstrations, the help of French industrial enterprises. The seminar had been a great success; 63 people had taken part, coming from numerous Member States, from one Corresponding Member (Albania), and from one country which was not a member (Saudi Arabia). A detailed account of the proceedings had been published in the OIML Bulletin of September 1987.

Mr THULIN added that with the seminars of 1981,1983,1985 and 1987 a start had been made on all the most important subjects in legal metrology, but no doubt a return to some of them could be justified by the technical progress that had been made. For September 1989, for example, another seminar on weighing could be organised, perhaps on the checking of electronic instruments.

Mr KOCHSIEK said that the PTB was prepared to cooperate with the Bureau in organising this seminar, which could be about the application of the RI on Non-automatic weighing instruments , of which a new version would be approved by the Eighth Conference.

Mr BENKIRANE asked that thought be given to organising a seminar on the setting up and running of laboratories for calibration and testing, a subject which was currently of great importance to developing countries.

Mr THULIN supported that proposal and thought that this type of seminar could be organised at regional level, as was already being done, for example, in the framework of the Asia-Pacific Metrology Programme (APMP). The Bureau could try first to organise such a seminar for the French-speaking countries of Africa with the help of the industrialised countries concerned.

Mr BIRKELAND, in closing the discussion, encouraged the Bureau to implement the two proposals that had been put forward.

## 12. REPORT ON THE ACTIVITIES OF BIML

The Committee noted a report, of which copies were distributed during the meeting, which described in broad terms the work of BIML since the preceding meeting of the Committee in April 1986.

## 13. LIAISON WITH OTHER INTERNATIONAL INSTITUTIONS

a) Mr ATHANE spoke about cooperation between BIPM, IEC, ISO and BIML, in particular about the revision of the VIM and about the work in progress on the expression of uncertainties, for which the working group had met twice at BIML.

Mr ISSAEV added some remarks about the production of an ISO document on the metrological aspects of standardization.

b) M. PETIK reported on the metrological aspects of the « meeting of government officials responsible for standardization policy » in the United Nations Economic

Commission for Europe (UN-ECE), and on the part played by the Bureau. He said that reports on the work of OIML, especially that of SPs 21, 22 and 23, would be drafted, with coordination by the Bureau, for a seminar to be organised by UN-ECE in June 1988.

## 14. TERMINOLOGY

On Mr PODGORSKI's behalf Mr REFEROWSKI sait that Poland, who had general responsibility for OIML's work on terminology, agreed with the Bureau that, at least for the time being, a decision to maintain the status quo be adopted: keep the existing terminology secretariats until the completion of their work, no new terminology secretariats to be formed, but each Reporting Secretariat working on a given instrument to be responsible for including in the RI's or DI's concerned the specific definitions needed for the understanding of these texts. Clearly these specific terminologies would have to conform to the general rules established by SP 1-Sr 2.

Mr REFEROWSKI added that Poland had no objection to the adoption by the Committee, in accordance with the rules applicable to International Documents, of the VML and other vocabularies in course of preparation.

Mr BIRKELAND thought that this question could be settled along the lines suggested by Poland, and he instructed the Bureau to submit a final report to the Committee within an appropriate time.

## CLOSURE

Mr EDGERLY announced that as a result of his appointment as head of another division of the NATIONAL BUREAU OF STANDARDS he would have to give up the positions he held within OIML. He spoke of the pleasure that his long association with the work and development of OIML had given him over many years, and of his hope that the Organization would continue in its successful path. He introduced Dr S. CHAPPELL, the USA's new representative on the Committee.

Messrs BIRKELAND and ATHANE both expressed keen appreciation of the role that Mr EDGERLY had played in OIML's development since 1972, the year in which the USA joined OIML, first in support of the USA's representatives in the Committee, then as himself Member and Vice-President of CIML. They stressed how Mr EDGERLY's role had been essential not only in enlisting the participation of American industry in the work of OIML but in improving the working methods of the Organization itself.

Finally Mr BIRKELAND thanked all his colleagues on the Committee for their active participation in that Twenty-Second Meeting; and in the name of all those present he also expressed his appreciation of the efforts of all who had contributed to bringing it about.
