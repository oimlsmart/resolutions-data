Mr. Mondher Zenaidi, Tunisian Minister of Commerce

Mr. President of CIML, Mr. Director of BIML, Representatives of OIML Member States Honorable guests, Ladies and Gentlemen,

Allow me first of all to express my pride and happiness in delivering the inaugural speech at this, the 34th CIML Meeting, that is this year being held in Tunis. I would like to take this opportunity to express my gratitude to the OIML for having chosen Tunisia to host its meeting.

My thanks equally go to the organizers of this meeting, which undoubtedly constitutes an appropriate opportunity for all those concerned with legal metrology to study issues related to this matter due to their importance for consumers and enterprises alike.

Over the past years the world has witnessed important developments consisting mainly of the emergence of globalization of the economy, regional gatherings and important scientific developments, especially in the technological field and other areas including telecommunication and computer sciences. The phenomena of economic globalization, fierce competition and the opening up of international markets allow only products of a very high quality produced at lower costs to enter markets in the light of a new global economy in which high quality and economic efficiency constitute the cornerstones of exchanges.

In the midst of these changes, Tunisia has since the Change of the 7th of November and under the leadership of President Zine El Abidine BENALI, implemented an integrated program to liberalize and reform our economy by implementing a market economy system, liberalizing foreign trade, regulating internal trade and providing incentives for individual initiatives in industry, trade, agriculture and services.

All these reforms have led to the consolidation of economic productivity and to respectable

rates of development. Over the latest decade the GDP has increased by approximately 5% at constant prices and by 6% in non-farming sectors; exports increased by 7% at constant prices.

These reforms equally concerned legal metrology, whose importance nobody is oblivious to, as a means to protect consumers and promote domestic and foreign trade thanks to the use of accurate accredited measuring instruments and the use of scientific methods governed by law. His Excellency the President of the Republic declared during the inauguration of the second congress of the Tunisian Association of Industrialists and Traders (UTICA), on November 29, 1995 the updating of the framework of legal metrology which dates back more than a century and the enacting of a new law.

This new law was indeed enacted last May. It facilitated a better definition of legal measuring units, the organization of their use by adopting the international system of measuring units called SI. This system is characterized by the harmony between its component elements and a better definition of measuring units, and is equally presently adopted by most OIML Member States, including Tunisia. This system will supersede the metric system that has been abandoned by developed countries for some years now.

In addition to clarifying prerequisites of manufacturing, repairing and marketing measuring instruments, this new law has clearly defined the various types of legal metrology verifications such as recognition of pattern approval, initial verification and statistical control of prepacked products in addition to technical verification. The aim is to give the consumer the right to demand the verification of a measuring instrument should he or she doubt its ability to accurately assess the measured quantities.

In addition to all this a consultative body, the National Council of Legal Metrology, has been set up. It comprises representatives of all the

ministries concerned with measuring activities. Its tasks consist essentially in encouraging research in and the promotion of legal metrology, the development of training, a better dissemination of information and encouraging cooperation and the exchange of expertise between national and international organizations.

We have equally consolidated the legal metrology departments by providing them with competent staff, and have set up an integrated training program in addition to creating a technical branch at the National Institute of Applied Sciences and Technology to train technicians in metrology. The first group of students has graduated this year and they will undoubtedly contribute to the success of the upgrade program implemented by the Tunisian State thanks to the strong will of its President BEN ALI to introduce quality systems according to ISO 9000 and to monitor the role of metrology in enterprise as a prerequisite for high quality.

Besides, other measures have been taken to set up laboratories for the calibration of measuring and weighing instruments and containers to ensure that the verification tools used by metrology agents are accurate and in conformity with international standards. This is in addition to the preparatory work underway for the implementation of a national laboratory for the testing of measuring instruments, in conformity with International Recommendation R76 issued by your Organization.

Tunisia was among the first countries to join the OIML and to consistently take part in its activities. It has made efforts to consolidate the orientations of the OIML as it believes in the need to coordinate actions in this field at international level to facilitate transactions and to eliminate technical obstacles that hinder trade. Tunisia's chairmanship of the OIML Development Council is at the same time an honor bestowed upon our country and bears testimony to the good reputation Tunisia enjoys within international organizations. We will do our utmost to coordinate efforts with all the countries of this Development Council to implement its program so that these countries may keep pace

with the development that metrology is witnessing and to make the Council the best forum for those entrusted with metrological verification by giving them the opportunity to take part in training programs.

I would like to take this opportunity to hail the delegations from the shores of the Mediterranean who took part in the Round Table held on Monday for their efforts, the results they have obtained and the ideas they have put forward. This Round Table constituted a forum to study the appropriate solutions to the problems relating to metrology in general and the methods and ways of verifying measuring instruments from their design to their marketing nationally and internationally. This initiative equally constitutes a framework for setting up a cell of cooperation between both shores of the Mediterranean to promote the activities of metrology and the structures entrusted with legal metrology in this area. It undoubtedly constitutes another model for consolidating cooperation between the countries that took part in this meeting.

## Ladies and gentlemen,

In conclusion, I would like to pay tribute once again to the OIML for the tremendous work it has been doing to promote legal metrology at all levels and to provide experts with information and advice concerning this field in general and the methods of verifying the different measuring instruments in particular.

I would equally like to congratulate the Development Council for all its efforts and actions carried out for the benefit of developing countries, for training their experts, and for the consolidation of the infrastructures of the departments entrusted with implementing the legislation and recommendations concerning legal metrology.

My warm thanks go to all of you and may your meeting be crowned with success.

I equally wish all the participants a pleasant and agreeable stay in our country. Thank you for your attention.

Mr. Minister of Commerce, Ladies and Gentlemen, Dear Colleagues,

It is my great pleasure to welcome you to this the 34th Meeting of the International Committee of Legal Metrology and I would like to immediately express my sincere thanks to all those who, here in Tunis, have played a role in the preparations for this Meeting. I am sure that their efforts will be fully recognized and that, at the end of the week, it will be my duty and pleasure to express the deep appreciation of the OIML community for such successful arrangements.

This Meeting obviously presents a number of very special characteristics. One of these is the fact that, for the very first time, the CIML meets on the African continent and in an Arabic country.

Up to now we have held Meetings either in Europe, North or South America, Asia and Oceania but, with the exception of OIML participation in meetings or workshops organized for example by UNESCO in Egypt or by ARSO in Togo, we have never held an important OIML meeting on the African continent.

I must say that there are a number of converging signals that show that the African continent in general, and Maghrebian countries in particular, are step by step occupying a significant place in legal metrology cooperation: just a few months ago a number of OIML representatives, including myself, attended a commemorative meeting in South Africa; before that, at the end of last year, a workshop on OIML R 76 had been organized by SADCMEL in Zimbabwe; I note too the development of regional or subregional bodies. I do hope that these are all strong signs indicating the rapid development of metrology in this region.

The other specific features of this CIML Meeting are the following:

- for the very first time regional legal metrology organizations as well as regional bodies having activities connected with legal metrology have been officially invited to attend a CIML Meeting; of course, up to now, a number of CIML Members were in fact also representing certain regional

bodies at CIML Meetings; however, this invitation should not be considered as a formalization and an officialization of the situation; in fact, we would be glad that regional bodies consider it as an opportunity to develop their own inter-regional cooperation;

- also for the very first time, OIML Corresponding Members have been formally invited to attend a CIML Meeting; this is the proof of the interest that the OIML has in making sure that as many countries and economies as possible will benefit from its activities and participate in them according to their resources.

Apart from these new characteristics, this CIML Meeting will - once again - have to examine matters and to make decisions of significant importance for the life of our Organization.

Following the Birkeland Report that was finalized one year ago, the Presidential Council and the BIML have identified a number of actions which should be carried out rapidly or in any case over the next two or three years. Proposals were sent to you some months ago and I am confident that you have been able to carefully examine them with a view to accepting the proposed work plan, improved and supplemented in line with any comments you may have.

Matters of development must also be duly considered: yesterday, a meeting of the OIML Development Council was held under Mrs. Annabi's chairmanship, and we eagerly await the Council's report and proposals for future work.

Also on the agenda are the traditional items dealing with conformity assessment, mutual recognition of test results, certification, technical activities, and so on.

Concerning cooperation between the OIML and other international and regional bodies, as already mentioned, we will be having discussions concerning legal metrology activities at regional level. We will also have discussions concerning liaisons between the OIML and the WTO, with the participation of a representative of this Organization, Mrs. Liu, from the WTO Technical Barriers to Trade Committee, who will join us this afternoon or tomorrow morning at the latest.

A new item on our agenda deals with the activities of the Presidential Council. For the

sake of transparency it has been considered appropriate that the CIML President gives a report about the Presidential Council at each CIML Meeting. It will then be evident that the Council is an advisory body and that all decisions still remain the responsibility of the CIML except in urgent cases where the CIML President has to make immediate decisions according to the OIML Convention.

Since this CIML Meeting is the last one before the Eleventh International Conference of Legal Metrology, we will have to start preparing this important event and to consider financial and other matters which will result in decisions being made by the Conference.

Another very specific matter will have to be carefully examined during these three days: the preparation for the selection and appointment of a new Director to succeed Mr. Athané. Several months ago I sent you a proposal endorsed by the Presidential Council. During this Meeting I will inform you of the reactions I have received (of which there are very few), but do not hesitate to contact me should you wish to express your views.

Since I have raised the question of key human resources, I think that it is perhaps appropriate to introduce Item 4 of the agenda straight away, which deals with the CIML Presidency. You will thus have the possibility to reflect about the situation, to exchange your views during the coffee breaks, lunches and dinners, and to be ready for a discussion which will prepare you for the decision that you will have to make next year.

I myself retired at the end of last year as Director General of the Dutch Metrology Institute, but I maintain close links with metrology in general and legal metrology in particular through my new position of Advisor to the Dutch Government for these matters. My Government has also confirmed my role as CIML Member for the Netherlands, and I maintain close links with the NMi experts responsible for OIML affairs, which enables me to keep in touch with the various aspects of OIML activities.

As a consequence of this new situation, I can now devote far more time to the OIML than was possible up to last year, and I must say that I am very pleased with this situation considering the personal interest that I have in OIML affairs.

As you know, my present term will come to an end in December 2000 and the CIML must

start reflecting about what action to take.I have discussed this situation with some of you including the two Vice-Presidents,and I would like to inform you very simply that, if you so wish, I am ready and willing to continue as CIML President.

Re-conducting my position would also maintain the necessary continuity over a period during which there will be a significant change in the BIML's management.

However, the Committee may also consider the possibility of appointing a new President who will take over from me at the end of next year. If so, it would be my responsibility to start identifying possible candidates and to make it possible for you to make the right choice at the next Meeting. Anyway, you now have three days to consider the various aspects of this topic.

A similar situation exists concerning VicePresident Chappell, whose term will also come to an end next year. Under item 4 of the agenda I will give the floor to Sam so that he may express his views concerning his position.

## 1 Approval of the minutes of the $ 3 3 ^{r d} $ CIML Meeting

The minutes of the $ 3 3 ^{r d} $ CIML Meeting were approved without modification.

## 2 Member States and Corresponding Members

## 2.1 New Members - Expected accessions

Mr. Athané said that no new full membership could be announced, although the accession of Croatia as a Member State was progressing, according to information received by the Bureau. Concerning Corresponding Members, Mr. Athané reminded the participants that several countries had been admitted or readmitted (following their delisting for not having paid their subscriptions for over three years) since the last CIML Meeting.

Concerning expected accessions, Mr. Athané said that certain Corresponding Members were considering the possibility of joining the OIML and that the Bureau had recently received a visit from the Embassy of Uzbekistan in Paris, with a view to a possible accession.

## 2.2 Situation of certain Members

Mr. Athané said that two or three OIML Member States were late in the payment of their contributions to the OIML and could be automatically delisted according to the OIML Convention. However, contacts with the legal metrology bodies of these countries had shown that there was a possibility for them to pay at least a part of their dues. Therefore, no action should be initiated at this time but decisions should be made at the next Conference.

## 2.3 Implementation of the decision made by the Committee at its $ 33^{\mathrm{rd}} $ Meeting concerning increased participation of Corresponding Members in certain OIML activities

Mr. Athané reminded the participants that two decisions had been made by the Committee at its $ 33^{\mathrm{rd}} $ Meeting.

Concerning the possibility for Corresponding Members to attend CIML Meetings, Mr. Athané noted that five of them were represented at this Meeting. Several other Corresponding Members had manifested their interest but were unable to attend, mainly for financial reasons.

Concerning their registration as observers in certain OIML TC's/SC's, Mr. Athané said that two or three Corresponding Members had decided to benefit from this possibility in 1999 and that the situation would probably develop progressively in 2000.

It was therefore possible to conclude that the CIML decision had been well received by Corresponding Members but that its implementation was progressive and had not resulted in any excessive extra work either for the OIML TC's/SC's concerned or for the BIML.

## 3 Financial matters

## 3.1 Adoption of the Auditor's report for 1998

The Auditor's report for 1998 was adopted without comment.

## 3.2 Examination of the financial situation for 1999

Mr. Athané reminded the participants that, as usual, a paper setting out the expected expenses and receipts for 1999 as well as the corresponding decisions made by the Conference in 1996 had been sent to all CIML Members in December 1998. This paper showed that the financial situation of the Organization was safe and well in line with the Conference's decisions, provided that the 1999 contributions were paid on time.

Mr. Athané added that he had no further comments to make at this time, except that the payment of the 1999 contributions was significantly delayed compared with previous years, this being probably due to the fact that a number of countries were facing difficult economic situations.

## 3.3 Budget for the year 2000

Mr. Faber reminded his colleagues that this matter had been carefully examined by the Presidential Council and that a paper had been prepared by the Bureau following the conclusions of the Council's examination. These conclusions were that the 2000 budget as decided by the Conference should not be changed, i.e. that the almost 3% increase in the budget and (since there were no additional Member States) in the contributions, should be maintained.

Mrs. Annabi felt that as a conclusion to discussions held during the recent meeting of the OIML Development Council and in order to take into consideration the economic difficulties faced by most developing countries, the 2000 contributions should be maintained at the same level as 1999.

Following a comment made by Mr. Johnston, Mr. Faber pointed out that the OIML should be very careful concerning increasing its financial resources for the next four years but, at the same time, additional activities and responsibilities and a need to accelerate technical work would surely appear in the near future. Therefore, if a near zero-growth was to be decided for the next financial period, it would be safer and more productive to start from the 2000 budget as decided by the 1996 Conference rather from a lower budget.

Mr. Athané pointed out that when adopting the 1997-2000 budget, the Conference had accepted an almost 3 % annual increase with roughly 1 % to increase OIML resources and 2 % to balance inflation. At present, the annual rate of inflation in France was very low, of the order of 0.5 %.

Therefore, it would be possible for the Committee to decide to slightly decrease the 2000 budget to allow for the decrease in the annual rate of inflation, as provided for by the Conference in 1996.

Mr. Boudissa pointed out the difficulties faced by many developing countries and therefore strongly supported the views expressed by Mrs. Annabi.

Mr. Birch supported maintaining the budget as decided by the Conference since (as to be discussed under other items on the agenda) the total activity and responsibility of the OIML will increase in the future, responding to the pressures of globalization, privatization, market forces, competition policy, trade facilitation, and the development of regional organizations. The appropriate resources for the OIML should not be discussed before these long-term policy issues. There were very significant challenges for legal metrology resulting more from international than national pressures that had to be responded to first at international and global levels. It was true that resources of national legal metrology bodies were generally decreasing but, at the same time, many governments were willing to accept to finance actions of specific interest, and the OIML activity should be one of these. In addition, it should be recognized that the rate of inflation in France was perhaps not an accurate index of the increases in OIML expenses. For example, salaries could increase globally by more than inflation. To conclude, Mr. Birch said that if legal metrology was to maintain its position, it needed strong commitments from governments, these commitments being very much determined by the resources governments were prepared to put into it. Mr. Faber fully supported these views.

Mr. Magana pointed out that the decision to be made concerning the 2000 budget was a separate issue, which should not be too closely connected with a more general discussion on the financial needs of the OIML. In fact, the Conference's decision to allow the budget to be reviewed by the Committee if the annual inflation rate was significantly different from 2% was mainly intended to protect the OIML against a big increase in inflation. It was true that governments were trying to reduce their operating budgets; however, governments might decide to put emphasis on certain strategic actions, which was presently the case for metrology in France. Whether or not OIML activities should benefit from this kind of strategic recognition should be decided during the next Conference. Mr. Magana concluded by saying that he could accept maintaining the 2000 budget as it had been decided by the Conference, although he well understood the views expressed by representatives of developing countries.

Mr. Issaev supported the conclusions of the Presidential Council concerning the 2000 global budget but asked whether it would not be possible to maintain the contributions of developing countries at their present level. Mr. Faber replied that it was impossible to fix two levels of contributions, one for developed and one for developing countries.

Mr. Johnston asked whether it would not be possible to use the reserve fund (which is currently significant) to offset the increase in contributions. Mr. Faber replied that according to the OIML rules, the reserve fund should not be used to cover current expenses.

Following a vote, the 2000 budget was maintained as fixed by the 1996 Conference, with five negative votes from Algeria, Cameroon, Egypt, Morocco and Tunisia.

## 4 CIML Presidency

Note: Discussions under this item were held in the absence of BIML staff and were not recorded. Consequently, only summaries of information delivered by Vice-President Chappell and by President Faber are given below.

## 1) CIML President

## 9 Liaisons with international and regional organizations

## 9.1 World Trade Organization (WTO)

Mr. Faber introduced Mrs. Liu, Economic Officer at the World Trade Organization responsible for the secretariat of the Technical Barriers to Trade (TBT) Committee of the WTO. He remarked that this was not the first time that Mrs. Liu had been in contact with metrology and the OIML since she had already participated in the International Seminar on the social and economic role of metrology held in Braunschweig in June 1998. He also mentioned that in the plan of actions adopted the previous day by the Committee, cooperation with the WTO had been explicitly indicated as being of major importance for the OIML and since most trade transactions are measurement-related, there was a need for the OIML to identify those fields in which its activity might assist the WTO in reaching its goals.

## Mrs. Liu then gave a presentation on WTO/OIML cooperation and on the main WTO activities.

The OIML had been an observer of the TBT Committee since November 1997 and the two Organizations were enjoying a very good working relationship. The OIML had participated in several important events of the TBT Committee and thus had the possibility to present its objectives and achievements to TBT Members, including their impact on trade facilitation. The WTO was established in 1995 continuing the work of the GATT to provide trade rules to facilitate trade. The agreement on technical barriers to trade was one of those agreements under the WTO, aiming at ensuring that activities related to mandatory technical regulations, voluntary standards and conformity assessment procedures did not create unnecessary obstacles to trade. For the purpose of transparency, WTO Members were required to fulfill notification obligations and establish national inquiry points. Another aim of the WTO was to improve market access. The fact that the OIML worked to prescribe necessary accuracy of measurement results and measuring instruments could promote product quality and competitiveness. This was also viewed as an important aspect of the capacity building of

developing countries to facilitate the products entering into the global market. In the same way, the fact that the OIML was operating a Certificate System for Measuring Instruments was connected with the fact that testing, certification and conformity assessment procedures were important areas of the WTO/TBT agreement. These facts showed how the work of the two Organizations was connected.

With a view to developing an improved understanding of the role of international standards under the TBT agreement, a TBT information session was organized in November 1998. It aimed at keeping international standardizing bodies informed of ongoing discussions on international standards within the TBT Committee and at increasing awareness of WTO Members of the activities of these bodies. Presentations were made by ten international organizations, including the OIML, ISO, IEC, ITU, WHO, FAO/Codex, etc. There were questions to these organizations, mainly on the openness of their programs, transparency of procedures for comments and decision making, application of adopted standards, when and how special problems of developing countries were taken into consideration, and the coordination of activities amongst these international standardizing organizations. Concerning the OIML, the TBT Committee was informed that the directives for developing International Recommendations were in line with paragraphs L, M and N of the WTO/TBT Code of Good Practice for the Preparation, Adoption and Application of Standards. In the same way, the cooperative agreement between the OIML and ISO/IEC to eliminate any risk of divergences and reduce duplication of work, as well as the existence of a special OIML activity for developing countries, were appreciated. Cooperation in and coordination of technical assistance work of the WTO and of the OIML should be developed.

Concerning recent activities of the WTO/TBT Committee, Mrs. Liu mentioned the workshop on information exchange about transparency provisions of the Agreement, the information session on international standards, the symposium on conformity assessment procedures, preparations for the Seattle Ministerial Conference, and the preparation for the second triennial review of the Agreement next year, about which Mrs. Liu gave detailed information. She mentioned that amongst the various items on the TBT work program, those dealing with international standards, conformity assessment procedures and technical assistance present much interest for most WTO Members. In particular, concerning international standards, the TBT Committee urged relevant international organizations to eliminate outdated standards and to make sure that new standards are developed taking into account the views of all parties concerned, including those of developing countries. To this end, cooperation between the WTO and relevant international bodies as well as amongst officials and experts in non-governmental standardizing bodies at national level were found to be essential. The TBT Committee also decided to explore ways and means of improving participation by WTO Members in the work of international standardizing bodies and of informing those bodies of the Committee's view on international standards. This was the reason to organize the information session already mentioned. Since the TBT Agreement encouraged WTO Members to use relevant international standards and guides and since it was recognized that these standards provide the possibility to remove unnecessary barriers to trade, there was a general wish within the TBT Committee that the bodies preparing international standards should follow certain guidelines to ensure the transparency, openness and impartiality in the development of standards. In fact, the credibility of a standardizing body depended on its working procedures. As a result of these considerations, the TBT Committee was intending to look at three proposals, from the USA, the European Community and from Japan, relating to the definition of international standards under the TBT Agreement and the working procedures of international standardizing bodies. This would of course apply to OIML Recommendations.

Concerning conformity assessment, the TBT Committee noted the growing concern with respect to the restrictive impact on trade of multiple testing and conformity assessment procedures and that the principle of "one standard, one test" and if required "one certification, one time" should be pursued to facilitate trade and reduce costs. The recognition of results of conformity assessment implied complex conditions concerning confidence in technical competence which, in most cases, demanded objective benchmarks. Use of common procedures defined in e.g. international standards in the operation of accreditation, testing inspection and certification bodies would be necessary to achieve the required confidence amongst Members in the field of conformity assessment. The TBT

Committee noted the value of ISO/IEC Guides, although certain difficulties of implementation may exist, and decided to pursue further discussions on this subject. The Committee also noted that there was a growing interest in concluding mutual recognition agreements (MRA's) but that concerns had been expressed on possible difficulties and problems associated with MRA's for developing countries because of reasons of cost, transparency, opportunity to enter into negotiations, etc. The TBT Committee decided to look at this carefully and to review the role of regional and international systems for conformity assessment and how these systems could contribute to solving the problem of multiple testing and certification and to allow developing countries to establish operational conformity assessment procedures.

Mrs. Liu, noting that a part of what she had described was related to legal metrology work, appealed to participants in this CIML Meeting to coordinate with their national trade authorities so that their experience could come back to the TBT Committee. At international level, the WTO Symposium on conformity assessment procedures held in June 1999 was intended to allow the TBT Committee to have a deeper understanding of the following issues: (i) relevant international guides and recommendations for conformity assessment, (ii) the type of conformity assessment procedures and conditions for their application, and (iii) the various approaches for the recognition of the results of conformity assessment procedures and the role of international and regional systems for conformity assessment. Thirty-five experts from testing laboratories, inspection, certification, accreditation, metrology and standardizing bodies as well as regional and international systems were invited as speakers and panelists. The OIML was one of those invited bodies and Mr. Szilvassy gave information on the OIML activities with relation to the interpretation and application of international standards in the OIML and on OIML projects that take into account the needs of developing countries. In the Chairman's report on the Symposium, it was recognized that conformity assessment procedures should not create unnecessary obstacles to trade and, at the same time, it was noted that effective conformity assessment procedures are necessary mechanisms to establish confidence between the different players in the market place. The Symposium included four sessions, as below.

The first session was related to relevant international guides and recommendations for conformity assessment and it was recognized that ISO/IEC Guides are widely used by developed and developing countries; it is noted that it was important to develop guides reasonably quickly so as to meet the needs of the market place. It was also noted that while the same international standard could in theory be used by different players of conformity assessment, variations in interpretation could lead to different applications, due to e.g. language or geographical differences. It was suggested that international trade could benefit from conformity assessment procedures which were transparent, impartial and based on international standards.

The second session dealt with various types of conformity assessment procedures and their condition of application. It was noted that third party evaluation of conformity was widely used, supported in many cases by national accreditation systems. It was used for conformity assessment to both voluntary standards and to mandatory technical regulations. Matters such as the harmonization of inconsistent accreditation procedures or a code of good practice for conformity assessment bodies were discussed.

Session three dealt with approaches with respect to the recognition of the result of conformity assessment procedures. It was noted that numerous mutual recognition agreements had been concluded and that several different types of MRA's existed. The numerous benefits of MRA's were stressed but it was explained that they were not the only option for addressing recognition issues and might not even be the appropriate option in a number of cases. The cost/effectiveness of MRA's was recognized as an issue which should be carefully addressed. Some concerns were also expressed concerning the discriminatory trade effect of some governmental MRA's.

The final session dealt with the role of international and regional systems for conformity assessment. It was found that international guides are commonly used by these systems, which play a useful role in coordinating conformity assessment bodies and through which technical assistance and know-how may be transferred to developing countries. It was however noted that it was necessary to eliminate duplication of work among different systems at all levels. These discussions on conformity assessment were prolongated at the level of the TBT Committee during a recent session. There were several proposals from WTO Members concerning the use of guides or codes of good practice that should be looked into concerning e.g. accreditation bodies or the negotiation of MRA's.

All this was related to legal metrology work and this is why Mrs. Liu again suggested that CIML Members should coordinate with national trade authorities in order to introduce their viewpoints concerning conformity assessment procedures.

Mrs. Liu then evoked the third element of the triennial review, dealing with technical assistance. The TBT Committee noted that certain WTO developing country Members encountered difficulties in the implementation and operation of the TBT Agreement and in the preparation of technical regulations, the establishment of national standardization bodies, regulatory bodies and bodies for conformity assessment. The methods by which technical regulations of other Members could best be met, the participation in international standardizing bodies, and the access to systems for conformity assessment were discussed. Technical assistance should be provided to requesting Members, particularly least developed Members, who were advised to inform the TBT Committee of any difficulties there were facing and assistance they might need. Other Members were invited to contribute to the technical assistance process by showing their experience in the implementation of the TBT Agreement.

Concerning the Seattle Ministerial Conference in November 1999, Mrs. Liu said that a number of Members had made proposals on TBT matters.

To conclude, Mrs. Liu noted the importance of obtaining as wide a participation as possible of all interested bodies in the discussions of the TBT Committee, including experts in metrology, the importance of which was now increasingly recognized in relation to trade, although much progress had still to be made.

Mrs. Liu replied that she was not aware of this agreement. She pointed out that the difficulties she had mentioned were mainly related to bilateral MRA's, which seemed not to be the case for the agreement to be signed within the Meter Convention, which was probably a multilateral MRA remaining open to additional signatories after the initial conclusion.

Mr. Schulz noted that there were two sets of standards related to quality management, one being the ISO 9000 series and the second being ISO 17025 and other similar Guides. One set was operated by accreditation bodies, the second by certification bodies. There was an increasing overlap regarding quality management requirements. Industry, especially medium-sized industry, was unhappy about this situation. Mr. Schulz asked Mrs. Liu what the views of the TBT Committee were concerning this and what the TBT Committee's action could be to avoid this kind of overlapping and competition between certification and accreditation bodies in the future.

Mrs. Liu replied that the WTO was not (and would not be) involved in writing international standards. Problems arising from the coexistence of the ISO 9000 series and ISO 17025 were to be solved by ISO, which had to take care of the interests of all the bodies concerned. She was well aware of the problem mentioned and hoped that ISO would be able to solve it. However, the WTO/TBT Committee clearly wished that international standardizing bodies would not compete with each other and that the development of International Standards and Guides would take care of market needs, trade interest, and also the interests of the various bodies involved. To this end, the TBT Committee was thinking about establishing guidelines for international standardizing bodies.

Mr. Boudissa, noting that agreements between developed countries such as the USA and the European Union had been discussed over several years before being signed, expressed the fear that for developing countries, the situation would be even worse owing to the difficulties these countries are facing to comply with the rules specified by the WTO. Technical assistance was necessary to allow developing countries to save time and money and to be able to trade both between themselves and with more developed countries. He therefore asked Mrs. Liu what the WTO had done and was going to do in this field and what donor agencies or countries could bring in terms of financial assistance concerning conformity studies for developing countries.

Mrs. Liu replied that technical assistance was always an important part of the WTO's work. The technical Cooperation Division was responsible for this assistance in general and, in addition, each division was organizing technical cooperation work. Under the Agreement itself, there was an obligation for Members to provide technical assistance on a mutually agreed kind of condition. It was noted that many developing countries had problems complying with the technical requirements and fulfilling the conformity assessment procedures applicable in developed countries. At the same time, in the Agreement there are provisions giving the possibility for all Members to prepare regulations and standards to protect consumers, the environment, animals and plants in their countries, provided that when preparing technical regulations they do not discriminate and create unnecessary obstacles to trade, that they apply transparency procedures providing opportunity for comments, and that they use

international standards as far as possible. Developed countries should therefore not discriminate against developing countries and create unnecessary obstacles to trade for the latter when preparing such regulations and standards. It was obvious that developing countries require technical assistance and the WTO and other international organizations such as the OIML were doing their best to try to help them to be better equipped to compete in the global market. For example, the WTO organizes three annual seminars for developing countries in fields such as the use of international standards, the importance of product quality, standards information and improving conformity assessment activities.

Mr. Birch expressed his concern for the fact that the debate on technical barriers to trade was dominated by voluntary standards. Trade facilitation through legal metrology was less a matter of standards than of having a "system" in place, including an internationally harmonized system of units of measurement, measurement standards, sets of procedures, etc. which all together provide integrity of the "system". This matter of integrity was a basic one, as demonstrated during a recent Seminar on Software: there is a risk that the integrity of measurements can be harmed by manipulation of software. ## 9.5 Regional organizations having activities connected with legal metrology (in particular: European Commission and CEN/CENELEC)

Mr. Athané summarized the relationship between the European Commission and the OIML in relation to the development of a European Directive on Measuring Instruments (at present at the stage of a draft). Through his participation in a number of meetings in Brussels and with the active support of Representatives of Legal Metrology Services of the European countries concerned and of WELMEC, it has been possible to make sure that there were no significant divergences between the requirements in the MID and those in the relevant OIML Recommendations. This was necessary in order to make sure that instruments meeting OIML requirements would not be rejected systematically for metrological reasons when submitted to European type approval (this applying of course only to recently published OIML Recommendations). Following these discussions concerning the compatibility between the MID and OIML Recommendations, the European Commission had accepted to introduce in the MID two ways to obtain a presumption of conformity with the requirements in the MID: (i) European Standards to be developed by the European standardization bodies, CEN and CENELEC, upon mandates given by the Commission and (ii) Normative Documents developed by the OIML. For each instrument covered by the MID, the Normative Document would include the text of the relevant OIML Recommendation plus the requirements of the MID with, in parallel, the number(s) of the clauses of the Recommendation which satisfy the MID requirements. Manufacturers would then have several possibilities to satisfy the MID metrological requirements: (i) to prove direct compliance with these requirements, or (ii) to prove compliance with the provisions of the relevant European Standard, or (iii) to prove compliance with the provisions of the relevant OIML Recommendation as reproduced in the relevant Normative Document.

Mr. Athané added that at the time of the CIML Meeting, certain aspects related to the development of OIML Normative Documents were not yet fully clarified but that things were progressing: within some months, it would certainly be possible for appropriate working groups, made up e.g. of European P-members of relevant OIML TC's/SC's and of experts of WELMEC, with BIML participation as far as necessary, to develop OIML Normative Documents for many instrument categories covered by the MID. For the others, it will be necessary to wait for the revision of outdated OIML Recommendations or the development of new ones.

Mr. Bennett, speaking on behalf of both the United Kingdom and WELMEC, added that he had no accurate information concerning the date at which the MID would be approved and published. He

also confirmed that most countries in the European Union had actually expressed to the Commission their willingness to have MID metrological requirements which would not be in contradiction with the corresponding OIML provisions. To this end, a WELMEC working group chaired by Mr. Magana was working hard to make sure that the annexes to the MID in which these requirements were given would be compatible with the relevant OIML Recommendations. He also clearly indicated that manufacturers would not be obliged to follow OIML Recommendations or European Standards: they would have the possibility to meet the MID requirements directly; therefore, it was a one-way compatibility: instruments complying with the OIML would comply with the MID but an instrument complying with the MID would not necessarily comply with the OIML.

Mr. Chappell asked whether the agreement with the Commission was a formal one, with some paper signed which would commit the OIML as a whole, or an ad-hoc one, involving certain OIML Member States and to a certain degree the BIML, without any definitive commitment for the OIML. He also asked whether a similar or different, formal or ad-hoc, agreement would have to be concluded with CEN and CENELEC.

Mr. Magana replied that this recognition by the European Commission of the status of OIML Recommendations resulted in part from the fact that the OIML was recognized by the WTO as being an international standardizing body. Therefore, what will be recognized by the Commission as Normative Documents will be documents officially developed and formally approved by the OIML, not documents developed by a European working group on the basis of OIML Recommendations.

Mr. Faber confirmed that for the time being, there was no official agreement between the OIML and the European Union concerning this cooperation.

Mr. Bennett added that it was appropriate to differentiate the two ways for obtaining presumption of conformity to the MID. In the case of a European Standard, there would be a contract between the Commission and CEN and/or CENELEC whereby the Commission would issue a mandate to produce a Standard which, when produced, would automatically be considered as giving presumption of conformity with the MID. In the case of OIML Normative Documents, the MID made provisions for a Measuring Instrument Committee, comprising representatives of Legal Metrology Services of countries of the European Union, to look at OIML Normative Documents and establish whether they met the MID requirements. There would be no formal contract in this case, but a confirmation by representatives of EU countries would be necessary.

Concerning cooperation with CEN and CENELEC, Mr. Athané said that good contacts had existed a number of years ago when, CEN and CENELEC having received a mandate from the Commission to produce a European Standard on nonautomatic weighing instruments, the preliminary work had been performed by the BIML at no charge for CEN/CENELEC which had received a text in English and in French, based on OIML R 76-1, with a view to its direct approval as a European Standard (EN 45501). Initially, the BIML had not accepted to pay it, arguing that the European Standard had been developed by European experts who are also working for the OIML and that no copyright had been requested by the OIML to reproduce R 76. Finally, the BIML had accepted to pay in order to allow the revision of R 75 to start immediately and, at the same time, the BIML Director had suggested meeting with the Secretaries General of CEN and CENELEC with a view to reexamining the cooperation between their Organizations and the OIML, especially in the framework of the development of European Standards/OIML Normative Documents related to the MID. The matters to discuss would include whether or not the Commission would give a mandate to CEN/CENELEC to develop a European Standard in a field where an OIML Normative Document would exist, whether a European Standard and a Normative Document covering the same instrument should be identical, whether the OIML would request a copyright from CEN/CENELEC to use already existing Recommendations, etc. Mr. Athané was also intending to suggest that CEN/CENELEC develop European Standards covering not

## 10 Activities linked with mutual recognition of test results, accreditation, etc.

Mr. Chappell reminded the participants of the report he had given one year ago in Seoul concerning activities connected with a mutual acceptance agreement on OIML pattern evaluations. The efforts started in April 1998 through a small ad-hoc working group and, when the work was considered mature enough, it was transferred to TC 3 which met in June 1999 in Paris. As a result of discussions within TC 3, it was considered advisable to create a new subcommittee TC 3/SC 5 (see item 11.1) which would be responsible for a number of activities connected with mutual recognition of OIML pattern evaluation, including the work up to now under the responsibility of $ \mathrm{T A G}_{c e r t} $ and that on measurement uncertainty in legal metrology.

Mr. Chappell pointed out that this OIML activity was obviously connected with that being developed within the Meter Convention, since the equivalence of national measurement standards and the recognition of calibration certificates issued by national measurement laboratories were the basis for a further recognition agreement on the equivalence of measurement results in legal metrology.

Using transparencies, Mr. Chappell described in detail the content of the current draft agreement on mutual acceptance of OIML pattern evaluations, which is intended to be a voluntary system applying to instruments covered by the OIML Certificate System. The agreement will include OIML tests but possibly also some additional or different tests which could be required by some Member States for national pattern approval. The issuing of certificates will be restricted to Member States that

have declared themselves as issuing OIML certificates but the recognition will be open to any volunteering Member State. The paper will aim at creating the conditions of recognition of such certificates, thus permitting the operation of a mutual acceptance agreement through which any participant will accept and utilize the results of pattern evaluation test reports certified by any other participant and assume any legal responsibility associated with the use of the results, and in which the mutual confidence among participants is established through means of conformity assessment of each participant.

There will be an individual agreement for each category of instruments covered, with the possibility for each Member State to participate only in agreements of its choice. Mutual confidence will be established as follows: (i) each Member State wishing to participate will have to complete a questionnaire on national capabilities for carrying out such pattern evaluations; (ii) after that there will be an accreditation or peer review of testing laboratories carrying out such tests and an evaluation of the certifying body; (iii) supplementary elements will be further information on capabilities, training of testing laboratory personnel, exchange of test data and intercomparisons. Only two OIML Member States will be sufficient to initiate an agreement by informing the BIML so that other potential participants may apply through the BIML, which will be responsible for distributing appropriate information. There will be an appeal process and agreements will be in effect for four-year periods, with the possibility to review them periodically by mutual agreement of all participants. Basic documents to be used in the process will be ISO/IEC 17025 and ISO/IEC Guide 65 (with the OIML documents for application to legal metrology, the development of which is starting within TC 3/SC 5) and a paper on peer evaluation based on an EA document.

In reply to questions from Mr. Kildal, Mr. Chappell said that the draft agreement on mutual acceptance of OIML pattern evaluations should be ready by the end of 1999. Concerning the operational costs of conformity assessment of laboratories carrying out pattern evaluations (through accreditation or peer evaluation), he said that he had no estimate at this time; however, it should be understood that the cost will be borne by the participating laboratories. He also added that the BIML would be responsible for assembling the teams for peer evaluation and of course costs will vary according to the geographical origin of peers.

Mr. Beard asked whether accreditation bodies would be designated by the OIML or ILAC-related. Mr. Chappell replied that the participating organization would identify the accreditation bodies having an international or regional recognition (which would mean in most cases ILAC-related, without this being a strict requirement). A matter to carefully examine would be whether these accreditation bodies have the appropriate legal metrology experts. Mr. Chappell added that in certain Member States the national legal metrology services would find it unacceptable to be accredited by the accreditation bodies of their countries and would then prefer the peer evaluation procedure.

Mr. Beard also asked whether the rules for laboratories and certification bodies would be covered by an independent and complete OIML paper or related to ISO 17025 and ISO/IEC Guide 65. Mr. Chappell confirmed that this would take the form of documents of application of 17025 and 65 to legal metrology activities.

Mr. Laamoumri asked whether it was possible to implement existing recognition agreements in the field of legal metrology. He also asked whether the OIML had participated in the revision of Guide 25 to produce ISO 17025. Mr. Chappell replied that the OIML had not participated in the establishment of any mutual recognition agreement and what the OIML was establishing would be an independent agreement, the principles of which would be based on those implemented within more general existing agreements. Concerning the revision of Guide 25, Mr. Athané said that the OIML had participated with a view to ensuring that this document would not contain elements which would be inapplicable in the field of legal metrology.

Mr. Chappell replied that a working paper had been prepared by Mr. Lagauterie and a draft would be distributed for examination within TC 3/SC 5 at a later stage. Concerning uncertainty in legal metrology, Mr. Chappell replied that he would give oral information later. Concerning the CASCO draft on peer evaluation, Mr. Chappell replied that he was not aware of this work but that the OIML working paper which would be distributed soon about this was based on an EA document.

Mr. Chappell then gave information concerning the revision of R 87 on the net content of packaging under TC 6 responsibility. Within TC 3, the subcommittee SC 4 on statistical methods had recently been reactivated under a German secretariat and had indicated its interest in being involved in the revision of R 87. ## PR3

Concerning the draft revision of R 49 Water meters intended for the metering of cold potable water, participants were reminded that at the Tenth OIML Conference in 1996, a draft revision of R 49 had been presented after a rather favorable postal vote; however, the Conference, considering that there were still some risks of divergences between the OIML text and the corresponding ISO Standard

which was being revised, had requested OIML TC 8/SC 5 to cooperate closely with ISO in order to eliminate any divergence and to produce a new draft that the CIML would approve. It was also pointed out that since then, OIML TC 8/SC 5 had produced two draft revision of R 49: one covering only mechanical meters, and a second one also covering electronic meters. However, the P-members had not been able to decide which draft should be submitted for approval by the CIML and therefore, both drafts were submitted to the CIML directly, without an informative postal vote having been carried out previously. It was proposed to the CIML to proceed first with a formal vote of the draft covering electronic and mechanical meters, with the possibility to vote thereafter on the draft covering only mechanical meters in the case where the first draft had not been accepted.

Mr. Bennett, on behalf of OIML TC 8/SC 5, pointed out that there were still some differences between the OIML provisions for mechanical meters and the corresponding ISO provisions, but that these differences were not creating any conflict and that the two documents would be entirely compatible in their application. He also said that for the time being, ISO work was limited to mechanical meters but that it could be expected that in the future ISO experts would accept the views of the OIML concerning electronic meters. Therefore, there was no reason not to immediately approve the more complete of the two OIML drafts.

Mr. Chappell expressed his total disagreement concerning a parallel which had been drawn by Mr. Athané between this draft and the draft revision of OIML R 65 (see below). He objected to the procedure followed for submitting this draft to CIML approval since no "consensus process" had been carried out. He also pointed out that the US experts had been very active in the revision of R 49 which was a very important text; however, it would be an inappropriate precedent to have a draft Recommendation not go through the process specified in the OIML work directives, since the texts presented to the CIML were different from those which had been presented to the Tenth Conference. Therefore, the USA could support neither of the two drafts.

Mr. Bennett replied that the fact that the new drafts were different from the 1996 one resulted from the Conference's request to have the OIML text in line with the ISO one. In addition, the time schedule which had been observed was exactly that which had been accepted at the level of TC 8/SC 5; there was therefore no reason for the USA to reject now what had been accepted previously.

Mr. Chappell said that there had been sufficient time at the beginning of the year to decide which draft should be submitted for approval and to send it to CIML Members for postal examination and vote before submission to this meeting. The TC 8/SC 5 secretariat had initially followed this process but was unable to take a decision concerning which draft should be selected and then waited until it was too late to send it to the CIML for a decision to be made by correspondence. Therefore, Mr. Chappell considered that all of this constituted a violation of the work directives.

Mr. Birch said that there was a need, well expressed at the APLMF level, to produce a Recommendation on water meters as quickly as possible which would be compatible with ISO work. In fact, cooperation between the two Organizations was very good and the work of both OIML and ISO subcommittees was so intense that in fact it was possible to develop and agree on rather new OIML and ISO texts covering mechanical meters. Concerning electronic meters, the ISO subcommittee was not able to work as quickly as the OIML one, this being due to the fact that general provisions for electronic measuring instruments and more specific provisions for meters for liquids already existed in the OIML. It would be a pity if adoption of the more complete OIML draft was delayed just because ISO was lagging behind the OIML. Finally, Mr. Birch pointed out that there was a need to accelerate the production of OIML Recommendations in fields of great priority and that new working procedures would have to be defined to this end. Therefore, although the way the draft had been put forward was unusual, it was the best possible way.

Mr. Lagauterie said that France, which had been one of the countries that had objected to the submission of the previous draft revision of R 49 to the Tenth Conference, was now fully in favor of both new drafts.

The CIML accepted to first proceed with a formal vote on the draft covering electronic and mechanical water meters, which was approved with four abstentions from Brazil, Canada, Ethiopia and Sweden and four negative votes from Japan, Poland, Switzerland and the USA.

## 11.4 Availability of OIML publications on paper, electronic media and via Internet; use of Internet within the OIML

Considering that the matter of the availability of OIML publications had already been discussed one year ago, Mr. Pulham preferred to first give information concerning the recent developments of the OIML web site.

In early 1999, the BIML had carried out a survey on the use of Internet in general and specifically of the OIML web site. The interim results were published in the April 1999 issue of the OIML Bulletin. At that time, 97 % of those who replied to the survey (i.e. roughly half of CIML Members) had access to Internet. It seemed that access to Internet was also progressing in developing countries - a positive sign concerning the possibility to rapidly transmit information through this media. However, the OIML had to be cautious and the continued transmission of information on paper should not be abandoned too quickly. The frequency of visits to the OIML web site was also increasing, as was the awareness concerning availability of certain OIML publications through Internet. The OIML web site had now existed for three years and was totally managed internally by the Bureau, without external assistance which meant that there had been no specific cost to be borne by the Organization. At the time of the CIML Meeting, the web site comprised approximately 25 pages on line including general information on legal metrology, role of the BIML, lists of Member States and Corresponding Members, latest news (announcements of meetings, new Members, etc.), and technical committees and state of progress of their work. There is also a data base of OIML certificates, a full listing of OIML Publications with the possibility to download many of them using the identification "members" and the password "riweb", and a page for the OIML Bulletin. The web site also includes links to sites of other international, regional and national bodies.

The site was becoming completely bilingual (English and French) with, however, certain pages common to both languages. It was also intended to have a Spanish version of the site with the help of the Centro Español de Metrologia.

Mr. Pulham concluded the first part of his presentation by thanking all those who had contributed comments and feedback and by requesting OIML Members to participate in the continued development of the site. To this end, a feedback form was to be posted on the site to allow all interested persons to easily send their suggestions. He also mentioned that in 1998 there had been a total of 8000 connections to the site and that the average connection rate was now 1000 per month, emanating from all over the world.

Concerning the availability of OIML publications, Mr. Pulham said that their distribution through the OIML web site or by e-mail from the Bureau would represent significant savings of time and of money. For the time being, 43 publications were available through such means, including all the Recommendations applicable within the OIML Certificate System, using software permitting users to retrieve an exact reproduction of the paper printed version. The possibility to gradually phase out paper printing of publications and only make them available on disk or through e-mail or by downloading them from the web site should be seriously considered, although serving Members at two levels should be avoided. Therefore, abandoning paper printing would not be possible before each Member was able to use electronic communication means.

Mr. Magana suggested that OIML TC's or SC's could open web sites to accelerate and facilitate their work. This was already used within WELMEC and was found to be very efficient, provided that a certain uniformity in the layout be achieved and that advertising or other commercial matters be eliminated from such web sites.

Mr. Athané mentioned the increasing costs resulting from paper printing and the material problems associated with the storing of paper versions of more and more numerous publications. He recognized that it was not possible to abandon paper printing immediately. However, it was perhaps time to start reducing the number of printed copies of each OIML publication, provided that the Member States which had the necessary technical facilities would accept to limit the number of paper copies they request from the BIML and to print the additional copies they need themselves on the basis of an electronic version provided by the BIML on diskette or through e-mail, or by downloading them from the OIML web site. As an example, Mr. Athané said that ISO (which had more members than the OIML) usually prints only 350 paper copies of each Standard, whereas the BIML usually prints up to 750 copies of OIML Recommendations.

Mr. Beard asked what would happen for the sale of publications to manufacturers, private laboratories, etc. if the paper copies provided by the BIML were to disappear. Would it be possible for Members to download OIML publications and distribute them freely at national level?

Mr. Athané replied that for the time being Members should avoid distributing free copies of OIML publications to manufacturers, private laboratories, etc., since these should purchase them from the BIML. Free distribution should be limited to within legal metrology bodies. However, it was possible to transform OIML Recommendations into national regulations or standards, the distribution of which at national level was as for other regulations or standards. It was possible for CIML Members to establish national sale points for OIML publications, the product of sales being reimbursed to the BIML once a year with a 20% deduction to cover the costs involved in the operation of such sale points. If in the future the situation concerning paper printing by the BIML was to change, sales procedures would also have to be reexamined.

Mr. Vaucher asked whether links to the homepages of Member States could be established. Secondly, concerning paper printing, he suggested that only the documents requested on paper by Member States should be printed. Mr. Pulham replied to the first comment by saying that such links were indeed established on the Members listing pages whenever the Internet address of CIML Members was known, and invited those who so wished to request a link to their Institution. Mr. Pulham also said that the Bureau would consider the second comment.

## 12 OIML Certificate System

## 12.1 General information

Mr. Kochsiek reminded the participants that a written report had been distributed to all participants concerning the various aspects of this item. In his opinion, the most important one was the activities of the newly established TC 3/SC 5 about mutual recognition and Mr. Kochsiek announced that he was ready to share the German experience about this with the participants but before that, he requested Mr. Szilvassy to give more information concerning the various topics under this item.

Mr. Szilvassy reminded the participants that the responsibilities hitherto under TAG $ ^{cert} $ would now be transferred to TC 3/SC 5 under the joint responsibility of the USA and the Bureau. He also mentioned that two new categories of instruments were applicable within the System: focimeters (R 93) and radiochromic film dosimetry systems for ionizing radiation processing of materials and products (R 127). The total number of Recommendations applicable within the System was therefore 28 and would increase even more with the publication of new Recommendations. As already mentioned under item 9, there had been good opportunities during the last twelve months to inform other International Organizations about the developments of the System. The revision of the operational rules of the System was starting with some preparatory work having been done by the Bureau and would develop within TC 3/SC 5. Concerning certificates issued, their number had reached 540 at the time of the meeting, for ten (out of 28) categories of instruments covered by the System. The list of certificates on the OIML web site had been supplemented with information concerning manufacturers that had been granted OIML certificates, and the Authorities that had issued these certificates. Last but not least, the revision of existing Recommendations with a view to their application within the System was progressing satisfactorily, including in the field of utility meters.

In order to show the importance of reaching an agreement on mutual recognition of certificates, Mr. Kochsiek gave a description of the various tasks of the PTB within which was being established a system for mutual confidence in the three areas fundamental for metrology: industry, regulated areas (following ISO 17025) and international cooperation (following the ISO 9000 series). Costs to establish such systems could be evaluated to 5 million USD. An experience had already been gained concerning explosion protection. According to regulations, the PTB was responsible for this field and needed a formal accreditation which, according to a European Directive, should be a full accreditation by a German accreditation body. However, this certificate was not recognized outside Europe because this German body was not known by non-European bodies. Therefore, a supplementary full accreditation was obtained from an accreditation body recognized by the European Accreditation body (EA). Nevertheless there were problems in e.g. countries where a single national and official accreditation system for explosion protection did not exist (for example, in one of these countries, there were three different and private systems). Therefore, the PTB had had to develop, in addition to its two full accreditations, seven MoU's or letters of intent to reach mutual recognition of certificates in some 70 % of cases where such certificates were needed. The associated cost was between 100 000 and 200 000 USD to have these two full accreditations and seven agreements with peer reviews. Therefore, the PTB strategy was to install a system following the ISO 17025 or 9000 series with self-declaration, but not to try to obtain full accreditation in all these fields, owing to additional costs. In the field of legal metrology, it would be possible to have a full accreditation from 2000, if this appeared to be necessary. As a conclusion, Mr. Kochsiek said that a full accreditation in legal metrology was useful only when the accreditation body had expertise in legal metrology.

Mr. Faber recognized that what had been said by Mr. Kochsiek was very important for the future of cooperation and mutual recognition procedures within the OIML. Since there was no time to discuss this matter now, it would perhaps be possible to decide to have a thorough discussion on

another occasion. Mr. Vaucher agreed that it was a key matter for TC 3/SC 5 and that similar discussions had taken place within the working group on a mutual recognition agreement within the Meter Convention: matters of cost and feasibility to establish confidence were essential and it was concluded that it was not possible to rely on one possible way such as accreditation (which was a good tool but sometimes inadequate), but to use self-declaration which would be considered as equivalent to accreditation when supported by the results of key-comparisons. In addition, each laboratory should have a quality system and a special role would be given to regional metrology bodies in the evaluation of the declared capabilities. Mr. Nyström noted that the problem mentioned by Mr. Kochsiek resulted perhaps from the fact that in Germany, there were two accreditation systems according to whether the activity was in the voluntary or regulatory field, whereas in most other European countries there was a unique accreditation system. Mr. Johansen suggested that this kind of discussion should be referred to TC 3/SC 5 and Mr. Faber agreed with this proposal.

## 12.2 New Recommendations applicable within the System

The Committee agreed that the new versions of R 60 (Metrological regulation of load cells) and of R 65 (Force measuring system of uniaxial material testing machines) would become applicable within the System as soon as published. It also agreed that the Bureau should distribute the draft Annex (test report format) for R 129 (Multi-dimensional measuring instruments) to CIML Members for postal approval as quickly as possible so that the complete Recommendation would be published and would become applicable within the System after approval of the Annex.

Concerning R 60, Mr. Athané reminded the participants that this was the first time that a Recommendation which was applicable within the System was revised; it was therefore necessary to decide on practical provisions in line with the general rules as fixed by the operational rules of the System.

It was first discussed whether it would be possible to continue to deliver certificates against the 1991 edition of R 60 whereas a new edition had been approved and would be published soon, and if so up to which date.

Mr. Magana said that it was necessary to accept this, since manufacturers were obviously going to apply for such certificates during the coming months. It would be impossible to reject such applications and, therefore, the possibility to issue R 60-1991 certificates should remain open for at least one year. In addition, the 1991 edition of R 60 should remain available (e.g. on the OIML web site) as a reference for the certificates issued against it.

Mr. Birch suggested limiting this period to the time necessary for applications already made or going to be made to be dealt with, i.e. maximum a few months. However, if discussions concerning the transformation of R 60-1991 certificates into R 60-2000 certificates were to be delayed until e.g. the next CIML Meeting, a longer period should perhaps be necessary.

Mr. Chappell said that he would agree with Mr. Magana's proposal, provided that clear information concerning the existence of the new R 60 and other information is given by issuing authorities to manufacturers, so that they may make appropriate decisions concerning application for OIML certificates.

Mr. Engler agreed with these views and suggested that two dates should be fixed: the date until which applications according to the old version may be made (e.g. between one and three months after the issuing of R 60-2000) and the date until which certificates according to R 60-1991 may be issued. Mr. Athané said that it would be difficult to fix two dates since the time between an application and the issuing might considerably vary from one Issuing Authority to another. In addition, time should be left to manufacturers and to issuing authorities, as well as to legislators, to adapt to the new R 60.

Mr. Birch said that the final date to consider was that after which it would no longer be possible to issue certificates according to R 60-1991 in order to avoid such certificates being issued for perhaps years to come. This date having been fixed, each Issuing Authority would fix the deadline to receive applications, based on the duration of their own testing.

Mr. Birdseye said that the critical date was that of the application, since the duration of testing could be indeterminate. In fact for manufacturers it was important to know up to which date they would have the possibility to apply and the end of 2000 - as proposed - seemed acceptable.

As a conclusion to these discussions, Mr. Faber suggested that the Committee should accept the proposal made by France and the BIML and supported by the United Kingdom and other participants, and the Committee decided that applications for certificates according to R 60-1991 could be sent to Issuing Authorities up to 2000.12.31.

Concerning the procedures for transforming R 60-1991 certificates into certificates according to the new version of R 60, Mr. Chappell said that a paper was being developed within OIML TC 9.

Mr. Athané suggested that the Committee should agree that an Issuing Authority having delivered R 60-1991 certificates could issue, at the applicants' requests, certificates according to the new version of R 60 after having carried out only the additional and different tests as described in the document mentioned by Mr. Chappell. According to cases, additional partial test reports or new complete test reports would have to be prepared. Fees for these additional and different tests and for test reports would have to be fixed by the Issuing Authorities and the new certificates would be registered after payment of the registration fees. Mr. Athané also said that the Bureau would carry out an inquiry in order to ascertain which Issuing Authorities would be able to issue certificates according to the new version of R 60.

Mr. Magana said that this matter could be dealt with very simply by the Issuing Authorities themselves, without the need for too many specifications from the Bureau or the TC. When a manufacturer has been granted a R 60-1991 certificate and when the unchanged load cell is tested and found to comply with R 60-2000, then the Issuing Authority might deliver a new certificate based on additional tests; however, a new and complete test report should be developed. If the load cell needs to be modified to comply with the new R 60, then the Issuing Authority should be free to decide which additional tests should be carried out and which tests should be repeated since a number of characteristics of the load cell might have been modified.

Mr. Birch said that information concerning the TC 9 paper and procedure for transforming old R 60 certificates into new ones should be distributed as soon as available; however, a final decision would have to be made by the Committee and this could not be done before October 2000.

Mr. Chappell said that the comments presented during this meeting would be considered by the TC 9 Secretariat when finalizing the document mentioned. However, final approval by the CIML could be done by correspondence without waiting for the next meeting.

As a conclusion, Mr. Faber noted that the Committee could accept the proposals of Mr. Chappell and of the Bureau.

The Committee expressed the view that there would be no problem for Mr. Faber to be reelected at the next CIML Meeting but considered that it was appropriate to allow some time for possible candidates to make themselves known in writing to Vice-President Chappell before June 2000.

The Committee, having noted that Vice-President Chappell was not willing to renew his mandate, decided that Prof. Kochsiek would become First Vice-President at the time of the next CIML Meeting and that, on this occasion, a second Vice-President will be elected amongst candidates who will have made themselves known in writing to President Faber before June 2000 or who will have been proposed by other CIML Members to President Faber within the same deadline.

## Terms of reference

The Presidential Council acts as an advisory body to the CIML President, especially for the following matters:

- Composition of the Presidential Council;

- OIML strategy and policy;

- Financial situation of the OIML;

- Personnel matters: positions of the CIML President and Vice-Presidents, BIML Director and Assistant Directors;

- Agenda of the Conference and CIML Meetings.

The Presidential Council can also advise the President on other matters.

## Periodicity of meetings

The Presidential Council meets at least twice a year, usually in February and in the Autumn (immediately before and/or after the CIML Meeting).

## Composition of the Council

- The CIML President and Vice-Presidents;

- The Presidential Council includes:

- The Chairperson of the OIML Development Council;

- Five to seven CIML Members, active and experienced, chosen by the CIML President so as to represent various interests (industrialized and developing countries, regions, etc.); these five to seven CIML Members are appointed as Presidential Council Members for a three-year term (usually starting when the CIML President is elected or re-elected) with the possibility of reappointment for three-year terms without limitation.

## Secretariat of the Presidential Council - Records of meetings

The BIML Director is the Secretary of the Presidential Council. He organizes the meetings and prepares the minutes to be distributed to Council Members and, as appropriate, to other participants in the meetings.

He also prepares a summary of discussions for the information of CIML Members. In addition, the activities of the Presidential Council must be the subject of a report given by the CIML President at each CIML Meeting.
